# Machiavelli is the most misunderstood thinker of all time – Ada Palmer

## Summary

Historian and novelist Ada Palmer returns to give the full historical context for Machiavelli's *The Prince*. She argues that early-16th-century Italy was uniquely unstable for two reasons: almost every city-state had recently had its thread of governmental legitimacy cut, guaranteeing wave after wave of overthrows, and the non-hereditary, roughly decade-long papacy produced an unpredictable new monarch every ten years, almost always an enemy of the last. Machiavelli's hope in dedicating *The Prince* to Lorenzo di Piero de' Medici was not that the Medici would unify Italy, but that they would consolidate enough power near Rome to make the papacy negotiate rather than knock over Florence like every other pawn. Palmer reads Machiavelli's fixation on Cesare Borgia ("Valentino") — at whose side he served the scariest diplomatic posting in Europe — as the emotional core of the book, right down to the moment the historian's third-person veil slips and he writes "he told me."

The middle of the conversation reconstructs the social machinery of the era. Palmer explains how proximity made Italians see "this Pope" (a specific jerk you went to college with) rather than "the papacy," producing wars where pro-papal cities fought the Pope himself. She lays out patronage as the glue of everything: 99 of 100 capital sentences were quietly commuted because the accused had a protector, Giordano Bruno burned only once he lost his patron, and the people of Rome literally rioted demanding *more* nepotism because a Pope's own son was the one commander guaranteed not to betray the papal armies. Cesare Borgia became beloved in conquered cities precisely because, as an outsider with no local faction, he delivered the first neutral justice in generations. Art, meanwhile, was cheaper-than-war diplomacy — a "culture victory game" — and antiquity was the era's cutting-edge technology, since backwards was forwards.

The final third turns to the book's afterlife. *The Prince* was proprietary technology, a job application written for an audience of one while Machiavelli chose to rot in rural exile rather than sell his statecraft to any foreign power — making the word "Machiavellian" a grotesque inversion of one of history's most selfless patriots. Palmer traces how Renaissance scholars had to smuggle original ideas inside commentaries on the ancients ("200 years of people being wrong about Plato"), how copyright was born from the Inquisition's book-licensing monopoly, and how figures like Machiavelli, Hobbes, and Spinoza each split into a real thinker and a useful villain-character (Old Nick). Sponsor segments for Cursor (23:05), Jane Street (46:31), and Crusoe (1:14:40) appear mid-episode.

## Key Points

- **Why Italy was uniquely unstable** ([0:25](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=25s)) - Most city-states had recently had their thread of legitimacy cut, guaranteeing repeated overthrows, while the non-hereditary papacy produced an unpredictable enemy-monarch roughly every decade
- **Stabilize, not unify** ([5:29](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=329s)) - Machiavelli wanted the Medici to conquer enough of a chunk that the papacy would fear and negotiate with them, not to unify Italy
- **The "he told me" moment** ([6:16](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=376s)) - When describing Borgia's fall, Machiavelli's third-person historian's veil breaks into first person, revealing how close he stood to Valentino
- **Fortune governs half of outcomes** ([13:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=801s)) - We should judge deeds by the most probable outcome *before* fortune intervened; the Borgias fell to luck, not to their choices
- **The means matter enormously** ([15:09](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=909s)) - Contrary to the "ends justify means" cliché, Machiavelli obsesses over *how* power is gained because that determines its stability
- **Lying is conditionally okay** ([16:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=986s)) - Savonarola's flip-flops destroyed a power base built on inspiration; Borgia's betrayals only made his terrified allies more loyal
- **First defense of multiple parties** ([20:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=1203s)) - Machiavelli is the first in the European tradition to suggest rival parties could coexist and vent tension rather than one exterminating the other
- **Bribing kings as an annual budget** ([22:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=1337s)) - Florence's standard tactic was paying off would-be invaders; the question was only whether Maximilian was a serious enough threat to spend on
- **"This Pope" vs "the papacy"** ([24:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=1455s)) - Geographic proximity strips the abstraction, so pro-papal cities could end up at war with a specific Pope from the rival faction
- **The most passive-aggressive letter** ([31:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=1881s)) - After the Pazzi conspiracy, Lorenzo sends his son with the message that he can't attend "since now I have no brother"
- **The papacy corrupts by generations** ([33:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=1980s)) - Church wealth compounds into a prisoner's dilemma where every family must manipulate the papacy defensively
- **The people demanded more nepotism** ([36:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=2184s)) - When Paul III appointed a competent general over his own son, Rome rioted, because only a son would never betray the papal armies
- **Patronage as the glue of justice** ([39:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=2340s)) - Cruel law codes commuted to fines 99 times in 100 through patrons; justice as a spiritual rehearsal of divine mercy, not proportional punishment
- **Bruno burned once he lost his patron** ([43:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=2604s)) - Giordano Bruno survived earlier Inquisition trials with protection; less-radical thinkers like Pico and Ficino were shielded by the Medici
- **Neutral justice made Borgia beloved** ([51:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=3060s)) - As an outsider with no local faction, Cesare Borgia delivered equitable justice for the first time in generations
- **Liberty is a system, even an unfair one** ([54:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=3240s)) - Machiavelli deems you a slave if any man can point and say "kill him"; even Florence's unjust system counted as liberty
- **Art as cheaper-than-war diplomacy** ([58:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=3523s)) - Florence couldn't out-arm France, so it played the "culture victory game" — art was statecraft, not surplus
- **Backwards is forwards** ([1:01:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=3681s)) - Antiquity was the cutting-edge technology; neoclassical buildings were high-tech aspiration, not nostalgia
- **A sin-and-repent culture** ([1:07:51](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=4071s)) - Pre-Reformation Christianity assumed everyone sins constantly; Saint Julian is the patron saint of murderers who repent
- **The Prince as a job application** ([1:18:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=4698s)) - Machiavelli chose to rot in exile and beg to serve the very regime that tortured him rather than sell his talents abroad
- **Books for an audience of one** ([1:24:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=5095s)) - Bespoke handbooks of princes written in a single copy were normal; the author's fame grew from the secret existing at all
- **Why The Prince surged into the canon** ([1:31:44](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=5504s)) - First as a tool to refute Hobbes, then in the 19th century as the founding text of church-state separation
- **Religion's psychological utility** ([1:39:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=5940s)) - Machiavelli and Thomas Paine both analyze religion as a tool for forming citizens, not as a state-endorsed truth
- **Original ideas couched in antiquity** ([1:45:12](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=6312s)) - Nobody wanted original ideas; scholars smuggled them into commentaries on Livy, Seneca, and Plato
- **Copyright born from the Inquisition** ([1:53:44](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=6824s)) - Book-licensing granted a monopoly to the printer who submitted to censorship; England later kept the copyright half
- **The Inquisition as Doctors Without Borders** ([1:57:25](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=7045s)) - It had no jails, funds, or arrest power of its own and was only as strong as the local government chose to make it
- **Old Nick vs the patriot** ([2:02:22](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=U1FrhkLQnCI&t=7342s)) - The villain-character "Machiavellian" split off from a real man of immense integrity, useful to us as a thought experiment
