# The Department of War is making a huge mistake.

## Summary

Dwarkesh Patel delivers a solo essay-style monologue responding to the Department of War's designation of Anthropic as a "supply chain risk" after the company refused to remove its red lines around mass surveillance and autonomous weapons. Patel frames this as a warning shot for the future: within 20 years, AI will constitute 99% of the workforce across military, government, and private sectors, making today's disputes over acceptable-use policies a preview of the highest-stakes power negotiations in human history. While he concedes the military has a reasonable case for refusing to use a contractor that retains a kill switch, he argues that the government's move to threaten Anthropic's existence as a private business -- by designating it a supply chain risk that could cut off relationships with Amazon, Nvidia, Google, and Palantir -- crosses a dangerous line.

The core of the argument is that AI structurally favors mass surveillance by removing the human labor bottleneck that has historically made it impractical. Patel calculates that 100 million CCTV cameras in America could be monitored for roughly $30 billion today, with costs falling 10x per year, making total surveillance cheaper than the DEA's annual budget by 2030. He warns that once the technical capacity exists, the only protection is political norms and laws -- and that Anthropic's refusal helps establish those norms. However, he acknowledges that individual acts of corporate courage are ultimately insufficient because open-source models will eventually provide the government with equivalent capabilities regardless of what frontier labs refuse to do.

Patel also dives into the philosophical paradox at the heart of AI alignment: perfect alignment -- making AI systems follow someone's intentions -- could produce "robot armies" that never question immoral orders. He draws on historical examples like Stanislav Petrov, who defied protocol and prevented nuclear war, and the East German border guards who refused to shoot during the fall of the Berlin Wall, to argue that models may need their own robust sense of morality. He critiques the AI safety community for naively urging regulations that could hand a "fully loaded bazooka" to future despots, and pushes back against the analogy of AI to nuclear weapons, arguing instead that AI is more like the Industrial Revolution -- a general-purpose substrate that should be regulated at the level of specific destructive use cases, not placed under total state control.
