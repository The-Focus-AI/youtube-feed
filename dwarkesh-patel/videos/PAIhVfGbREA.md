# Why Leonardo was a saboteur, Gutenberg went broke, and Florence was weird -- Ada Palmer

## Summary

Renaissance historian Ada Palmer joins Dwarkesh Patel to discuss her book "Inventing the Renaissance" and the multi-generational intellectual project that connected Petrarch's dream of reviving Roman virtues to the eventual birth of modern science. Palmer argues that the Renaissance was not a sudden rediscovery of ancient wisdom but rather a 200-year sequence of attempts, failures, and pivots: Petrarch believed that reading Cicero would produce virtuous leaders, his successors built libraries and raised "philosopher princes" who turned out to be just as brutal as their predecessors, and Machiavelli then proposed using history as a casebook of empirical examples rather than a source of moral osmosis. This iterative process eventually produced the Baconian scientific method and the idea that knowledge should be shared for the benefit of all humankind.

The conversation covers extraordinary ground on information revolutions, drawing direct parallels between the printing press and the digital revolution. Palmer explains why Gutenberg went bankrupt (he had 300 Bibles but only seven local customers who could legally read them), why his apprentices also went bankrupt, and how it took 40 years before printing was even economically sustainable. She argues that the printing press, pamphlets, newspapers, and magazines were not separate revolutions but successive applications of one information technology, much as computers, the internet, smartphones, and AI are successive applications of the digital revolution.

Palmer also delivers a striking reframing of the Library of Alexandria, arguing that most ancient knowledge was not lost in its famous burning but rather during the period 400-600 AD when papyri were physically crumbling and monks could only afford to copy a fraction of existing texts onto expensive parchment. The conversation closes with her account of how the Inquisition accidentally invented peer review by building laboratories to verify the scientific claims in books they were sent to censor.

## Key Points

- **Italian republics survived because of agriculture** ([0:33](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=33s)) - After Rome fell, wealthier Italian cities with good farmland could self-govern as republics, while poorer towns fell under feudal protection of local lords
- **Petrarch's dream of virtuous leaders** ([3:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=191s)) - Reading ancient Romans like Brutus who executed his own sons for treason inspired a project to recreate classical education and produce better rulers
- **The Medici used classical culture as propaganda** ([7:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=443s)) - As "merchant scum" with no nobility, the Medici surrounded themselves with Greek scholars, Roman statues, and Platonic philosophers to flip the power dynamic with visiting nobles
- **Florence massacred its nobles to protect the republic** ([30:14](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=1814s)) - After a near-coup, Florence eliminated its nobility and created a unique commoner republic ruled by lottery-selected merchant guild leaders locked in a tower for two-month terms
- **Consensus rule by nine random merchants** ([33:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=1980s)) - Florence's sortition system required consensus among nine randomly selected merchants, designed to be tyrant-proof
- **Cosimo corrupted the lottery through plurality control** ([45:10](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=2710s)) - As papal banker employing a third of the city, Cosimo statistically guaranteed a plurality on any random council would be loyal to him
- **Machiavelli's radical patriotism** ([51:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=3106s)) - When exiled, Machiavelli refused lucrative positions at foreign courts and sat faithfully in the countryside, dedicating The Prince only to his homeland
- **Gutenberg went bankrupt because distribution networks didn't exist** ([58:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=3516s)) - Mass-produced books in a world without distribution networks for mass commodities; Venice's hub shipping system was what finally made printing viable
- **Book fairs as the original distribution hack** ([1:00:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=3653s)) - Printers brought 1,000 copies of one book to fairs like Frankfurt, traded with 200 other printers, and went home with five copies each of 200 books
- **Print and digital revolutions are structurally parallel** ([1:01:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=3679s)) - The printing press, pamphlets, newspapers, and magazines were successive applications of one technology over 150 years, exactly like computers, internet, smartphones, and AI
- **Florence had 90% male literacy in the 12th century** ([21:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=1307s)) - Merchant culture required reading for letters and account books, but being literate and being book-literate were very different things
- **Leonardo and Brunelleschi were saboteurs of progress** ([1:10:25](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=4225s)) - Leonardo wrote in coded mirror writing and Brunelleschi burned his schematics; they wanted to be marveled at, not to advance collective knowledge
- **Francis Bacon's three insects** ([1:12:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=4320s)) - The ant hoards knowledge, the spider spins theory from nothing, but the bee gathers from nature and produces something "sweet and useful for humankind"
- **Ancient knowledge was lost when papyri crumbled, not at Alexandria** ([1:23:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=4998s)) - Between 400-600 AD, monks had to choose which texts to copy onto expensive parchment; their subjective tastes biased what survived
- **A medieval book cost as much as a house** ([1:24:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=5040s)) - Without papyrus, Europeans wrote on processed animal skins; the great library of the University of Paris had only 600 books
- **History has always moved fast** ([1:06:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=3960s)) - The video game Civ teaches that ancient turns were 50 years and modern turns one year, but zooming into any historical decade reveals equally rapid change
- **The Inquisition never caught printers** ([1:42:58](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=6178s)) - Printers employed news writers who moved information fastest, so they always learned about Inquisition raids before the authorities could arrive
- **Censors were always wrong about what mattered** ([1:52:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=PAIhVfGbREA&t=6720s)) - The Inquisition obsessed over minor Jansenist heresies while letting Voltaire and the Encyclopedie circulate; Machiavelli wasn't even in ALL CAPS on the banned books list
