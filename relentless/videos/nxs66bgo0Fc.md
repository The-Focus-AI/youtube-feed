# How we're going to power the AI data center buildout | Energy Sec. Chris Wright & Scott Nolan

## Summary

US Secretary of Energy Chris Wright and General Matter founder/CEO Scott Nolan sit down with Ti Morse for a sweeping conversation on how America will physically power the AI data center buildout. The framing: the US has barely grown electricity production in two decades while tripling oil and more than doubling natural gas — Wright argues this is "energy subtraction" by regulation, and lays out the Trump administration's reversal. They stopped the closure of 17 GW of coal plants this year (Wright says hundreds of Americans likely would have frozen during January's Storm Fern without it), are restarting Palisades, the Crane Clean Energy Center (Three Mile Island), and Duane Arnold for ~3 GW of returning nuclear, and are running 11 fast-tracked test reactors — the first non-light-water reactors to go critical in the US in 40 years — before July 4th.

In the immediate five-year window, gas is the answer. Wright details 100+ GW of gas turbines already under construction, gas reciprocating engines, and natural gas fuel cells finally scaling. He notes that 2-2.5% of US natural gas production is currently being curtailed in the Permian Basin alone — enough stranded gas to generate 15-20 GW of power. Nolan brings the venture-investor framing: a 1 GW traditional data center is roughly $10B, advanced data centers run $40-60/watt, and hyperscalers are now signing Trump's "ratepayer protection pledge" — paying above-market for power, funding 1.2 GW of generation for every 1 GW they consume, with multi-year nominal rate freezes. Indiana has already filed for rate reductions because of data center investment. Both speakers attack the "fixed pie" narrative: states like Texas, Nebraska, and North Dakota with fast demand growth have falling real electricity prices, while California, New York, Massachusetts, and Maryland have rising prices precisely because they shrunk supply.

The most striking exchange is the long-horizon math on nuclear enrichment. General Matter's first Kentucky facility comes online in 2030; together with the existing New Mexico European-consortium plant, the US will still only make half its own nuclear fuel. To supply US plus close allies under the Trump administration's 4x nuclear target by 2050, enrichment production needs to 30x. Nolan says yes, "this is America" — and points to SpaceX going from sub-20% of global launch capacity to 90%+ of mass-to-orbit in 25 years as the template, including his pitch for orbital sun-synchronous data centers as a wildcard for 100x energy demand scenarios. Wright closes with an explicit invitation: if you want to build big things in America and you're worried about permits, "call me up." The Department of Energy has 17 labs and millions of acres available, NEPA reform is now matched by a unanimous Supreme Court decision, and "Trump speed" is the new bar.
