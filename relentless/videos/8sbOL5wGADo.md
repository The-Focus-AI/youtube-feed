# The Manufacturing Startup That's Outcompeting China | Jim Belosic, SendCutSend | Round II

## Summary

Jim Belosic, founder and CEO of SendCutSend, returns to Relentless to walk Ti Morse through what changed in the eight months since their first interview — most importantly, a $110M raise led by Andrew, Matt, Patrick Collison, with Sequoia and Paradigm in the syndicate. SendCutSend bootstrapped for eight years on a "lemonade stand" model — buy lemons for 50 cents, sell for a dollar, reinvest 50 cents — and Belosic frames the new capital as a literal time machine: five years of organic growth compressed into 18-24 months. He waited so long to raise that he only had to give up 10% of the company, and he refused to take a CFO as a condition of staying frugal but generous.

Belosic argues that the magic bullet for outcompeting China is not automation — it's scale plus high-quality labor that actually cares. Today SendCutSend can meet or beat China on roughly 20-25% of parts; he wants 100%. The company's brand promise is built on speed (the cultural slogan in the factory is "3 PM is the new midnight" — if it doesn't make the truck, it doesn't count) and a ratchet philosophy: once you've delivered next-day, you can never go back to seven-day shipping. He talks about gambling on building leases and machine POs before signing — saving 30 days at the front compresses into millions of dollars of new capacity 12 months out — and his fear that the next bottleneck won't be capital but recruiting, training, and most of all American power infrastructure.

The interview closes on Belosic's vision: SendCutSend as the "Home Depot of manufacturing." Pre-Home Depot, you couldn't even walk into a lumber yard; pre-SendCutSend, you can't walk into a US manufacturer and ask for a part. He wants accessibility for hobbyists, formula SAE teams, and Fortune 500s alike, with vertical integration eliminating middlemen and bulk purchasing dropping prices. Belosic warns that US power infrastructure is the limiting reagent for reindustrialization ("we need a hundred times more power than we have"), that raw material prices never go down ("just look at gas prices"), and that the energy of the moment is largely "vibes" — patriotism, fear of offshore IP theft, and supply chain shocks that have made US manufacturing finally feel viable.

## Transcript

**Ti Morse:** [0:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8sbOL5wGADo&t=0s) Today I have the pleasure of sitting down with Jim Belosic, the founder and CEO of SendCutSend. SendCutSend is absolutely crushing building factories here in the United States and in my mind is one of the few manufacturing companies that is genuinely competitive with China. So with that in mind, I want to start off with what does it take for a US manufacturing startup to be competitive with China and is it even possible today?

**Jim Belosic:** [0:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8sbOL5wGADo&t=21s) Yeah, of course it's possible. It takes scale. You need a lot of scale. Manufacturing in the US right now is like I think 93% of it is companies that are 100 staff or smaller. And then like 75% of that is companies that are 20 staff or smaller. So lots of, you know, mom and pop type shops. You put them together and we're a force to be reckoned with, but no one is really scaling at the proper level to bring prices down, increase speed, whatever. So that's what we're trying to do. We compete with China on parts right now, you know, maybe 20% of everything we do, 25%, we actually can meet or beat China, but I'm not happy with that. I want it to be 100.

**Ti Morse:** [1:14](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8sbOL5wGADo&t=74s) Right now we have a bunch of machines in the background that in most cases are like semi-automated or a large part of the process is automated, but there is obviously some human touch. So how do we actually make, you know, companies like this competitive?

**Jim Belosic:** [1:25](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8sbOL5wGADo&t=85s) Yeah, automation is not the magic bullet. Fully automating and dark factories and lights out manufacturing or whatever, yes, it helps, but that's not the magic bullet. You know, we'll get there someday, but today's not the day. You have to have a mix. You know, we're really happy to have robots where it makes sense to have robots and then we have good dudes where it makes sense to have dudes and dudettes. Everyone thinks like, oh, because China has cheap labor or whatever, the only way to compete with that is to automate. What we've found is, no, you just have really good labor.
