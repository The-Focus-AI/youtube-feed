# When You Truly Work for Yourself

## Summary

Naval unpacks the central paradox of self-employment: when you truly work for yourself, you don't have hobbies, weekends, or vacations — but you don't have *work* either. The work-life distinction collapses. There's no 9-to-5, no office, no playbook, no one telling you what to do, and at the same time nothing to turn off because *you are the business, you are the product, you are the work*. The curse is that you can't switch it off; the benefit is that, done right and with the stress of missing goals set aside, it stops feeling like work and you become unusually productive — measured only on output, against a bar you raised yourself.

He calls this a one-way door: "a taste of freedom can make you unemployable." Once you've walked the tightrope without a net — even inside a small, self-motivated team — you can't go back to a manager, check-ins, fixed desks, and prescribed commute times. Nivi closes with a re-reading of the line itself: "when you *truly* work for yourself" can also mean *your labor is an expression of who and what you are*. Self-employment becomes self-expression — and that's not an easy thing to figure out.
