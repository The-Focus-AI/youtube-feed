# It Is Impossible to Fool Mother Nature

## Summary

Naval Ravikant discusses the importance of radical personal responsibility and converting potential into action. He argues that people who work hard, apply themselves, and take responsibility for outcomes on a long enough time scale inevitably succeed. Citing Richard Feynman's claim that he was "just a boy who applied himself," Naval pushes back on the trope of the smart lazy person, insisting that potential without kinetic application is wasted -- and that doing actually raises your potential because we are dynamic, not static, creatures.

The conversation shifts to Arthur Schopenhauer, whom Naval credits with giving him "complete permission to be himself." Schopenhauer wrote unflinchingly about human nature without caring what the masses thought, and Naval finds that permission to be exceptional -- to bet on yourself and acknowledge your strengths -- is one of the most valuable things you can get from reading. However, Naval warns against delusion: you don't get to declare yourself exceptional; that judgment comes from free markets and nature. Awards, critics, and social feedback are all fake because groups search for consensus, not truth. The central claim is stark: "It's very easy to fool yourself, it's very easy to be fooled by others. It is impossible to fool mother nature."
