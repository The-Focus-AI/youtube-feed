# Most Books Should Be Skimmed, A Few Should Be Devoured

## Summary

Naval Ravikant and Nivi discuss David Deutsch as the preeminent figure in modern epistemology. Naval opens by suggesting readers can skip everything else and jump straight to Deutsch for the state of the art on the philosophy of knowledge. Nivi pushes back slightly, noting that many readers first need to wrestle with traditional concepts like induction and "justified true belief" to fully appreciate how Deutsch dismantles them. He shares his own experience of initially treating Deutsch like just another physicist-author alongside Paul Davies and Carlo Rovelli, only to realize later that Deutsch was operating at a fundamentally deeper level with a coherent system where all pieces reinforce each other.

The conversation reveals how deeply interconnected Deutsch's theories are across physics, computation, and philosophy. Naval highlights a striking historical fact: Deutsch essentially invented the field of quantum computing as a byproduct of trying to falsify his multiverse theory. To design an experiment that could test whether wave function collapse is real, Deutsch had to imagine being inside the brain of a quantum AGI, which forced him to figure out how quantum computers would work in the first place. This chain of reasoning -- from abstract philosophy to the founding of an entire field of computation -- exemplifies how deep explanations in one domain can generate breakthroughs in another.
