# Find Your Specific Knowledge Through Action

## Summary

Naval argues that specific knowledge — the thing you uniquely do best, that aligns with who you fundamentally are — is not discoverable from the armchair. It's "life lived in the arena." You only find your superpower by acting in a variety of difficult situations and noticing either what you navigated through that others couldn't, or what other people start pointing at when they describe you.

His example is a serial entrepreneur friend who isn't the cleverest or most technical, and is hard-working but not uniquely so — his superpower is *courage*. "A hundred years ago you would have said, 'Oh, he's the most courageous, go charge that machine-gun nest.'" In entrepreneurship, that same trait shows up as the willingness to call 400 people and accept 399 no's for one yes. That single capability, applied where it's needed, makes him irreplaceable. The same logic generalizes: when you work for yourself, you'll naturally pick things and execute in a way that aligns with who you are — which is exactly how specific knowledge surfaces.
