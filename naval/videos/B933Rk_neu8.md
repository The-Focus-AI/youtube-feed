# The Best Authors Respect the Reader's Time

## Summary

Nivi opens by provocatively calling Naval an "industrial philosopher" -- someone philosophizing for wide adoption rather than the academy -- and argues that Twitter-style philosophy from people like Naval and David Deutsch has been far more valuable to him than Aristotle or Wittgenstein. Naval agrees, confessing he cannot stand most classical philosophers because they make obscure arguments over minutiae. The exception is Schopenhauer, whose shorter essays read almost like Twitter posts: high density of ideas, minimal examples, and paragraphs that leave you thinking for an hour.

Naval then lays out a strategic framework for reading. For understanding human nature, he recommends "Lindy" books that have survived the test of time. For developing specific, monetizable knowledge, he advises staying on the bleeding edge where information is timely but quickly obsolete. He explicitly avoids low-density works like most history books, preferring instead authors who compress wisdom into the smallest possible space. His list of high-density favorites includes David Deutsch, Borges, Ted Chiang, and early Neal Stephenson. The central claim is that "the best authors respect the reader's time" -- they deliver generalized principles and wisdom rather than drowning you in data.
