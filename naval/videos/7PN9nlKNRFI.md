# Good Products Are Hard to Vary

## Summary

Naval Ravikant explores David Deutsch's principle that "good explanations are hard to vary" and applies it broadly to product design, engineering, and the nature of learning. He argues that when a product or design reaches its optimal form, it becomes nearly impossible to change without degrading it -- the iPhone's form factor, the airplane wing, and the streamlined car are all examples of convergence toward a "Platonic ideal" where each component constrains the others so tightly that variation would only make things worse.

The conversation then shifts to the fractal nature of knowledge and high-density writing. Naval explains that good writers produce work so interconnected that readers will absorb different layers of meaning depending on their level of readiness. Learning is an iterative process -- you read something, grasp 20%, return later and get 25%, supplement with podcasts and AI tools, and gradually deepen your understanding. All knowledge is ultimately a communication between author and reader, and both must be at a certain level for the transfer to occur.
