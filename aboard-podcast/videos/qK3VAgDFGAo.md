# Craig Mod: Vibe Coding Towards the Apocalypse

## Summary

Writer, photographer, publisher, and self-described "software supertaster" Craig Mod joins Paul and Rich from Japan to walk through the personal finance and tax software he built for himself using Claude Code — and then to spiral, almost inevitably, into the apocalypse. Mod's situation is unusually messy: a US citizen living in Japan, running a membership program for his writing community, selling books printed and shipped from Japan (which makes him an exporter), juggling bank accounts in two countries and a tax treaty that means he files in both. Quicken couldn't handle any of it. So in January, he sat down with Claude Code and rebuilt his own version — a custom dashboard that ingests CSVs (via a headless Chrome Python script that lets him hold the login while it scrapes), categorizes transactions like Things app, runs a live tax estimate for the year, and tracks which forms he still owes. It took six days. The previous year, ChatGPT had already found enough errors in his accountant's work to make him fire the accountant.

The middle of the conversation is the most useful coaching for everyone outside the 5%. Mod is firm: do not put this on GitHub, because the bespoke fit only works because he programmed enough to feel where the seams are. He invokes Bret Victor's old line: "If I have given you everything you need, if you can't build from what I've shown you, giving you the code will not save you." Paul pushes back gently — neither of them is a great coder, but they understand the contours and genealogy of software, and that's the actual gating skill. They riff on Tailwind (Rich hates it; nobody looks at code anymore anyway), on a hypothetical "virtual blackboard" the size of a wall that would show the architecture of what's being built, and on Mod's friend in LA who's burning a thousand dollars a day in tokens with three Claudes running at once.

Then Mod swings the conversation: "This feels like it's 1938 and everyone has a nuclear reactor in their backyard." He's pessimistic about white-collar identity surviving when accountants, lawyers, and programmers — the verifiable, code-like jobs — all get eaten first. He floats the possibility that humans might not contain the genetic code to survive what they're building; that we might just be here to "hand off all our knowledge and then we disappear." Paul counters with the Aboard house view: ten years from now this office is still going, AGI isn't here, and "more and more human behavior spills out and it turns out we're not going to change." Rich argues the actual ceiling is human creativity, not the models — two years in, he hasn't seen anything exceptional come out of the prompt box. They close on a thread of cautious optimism: humans are messy and resilient; we're at version 0.001; in a hundred years we'll be at 0.0002 and it might be better. Mod plugs craigmod.com and The Good Place, his members-only Twitter with Ted Danson as the logo.
