# New Words for a New Industry

## Summary

Paul Ford and Rich Ziade abandon their usual guest format for a freewheeling two-hander about what to even *call* the work the AI era is producing. Paul opens by recapping a talk he gave to designers at Newsday where he confessed -- on stage -- that he doesn't actually know what the future of his industry looks like, and decided to compensate by inventing the dumbest possible portmanteaus. Customer + client = "clustomer" (already taken by MailChimp, sadly). Or "custolient." Designer + engineer + product manager = "deer duct." Product + agency = "pragency" (with a Y in there, P-R-A-Y, because that's where we're at). And the worst one: agency where the workers are AI agents = "agentrification." The bits land like a workshop set at the Gotham Comedy Club, but they're a Trojan horse for a serious conversation about boundary collapse.

Rich pushes the conversation toward the economics. The labor market sells two things, he reminds Paul: goods and services. White-collar high-margin work depends almost entirely on the "expertise delta" -- the gap between what an expert knows and what their client doesn't. Tax attorneys can charge $2,000 an hour because there are very few of them and the rest of us cannot read the IRS code. Radiologists can charge their fees because we cannot read our own X-rays. But that delta is closing fast: people are now going home at 8pm, asking ChatGPT for a contract review or tax strategy, and bringing what they got back into their next billable meeting. Rich's perception of his own tax lawyer's value, he admits, has shifted after one ChatGPT session. The natural reaction from incumbents -- "that's cute, but you can't put that into production / use it for diagnosis / sign your name to it" -- is reasonable but, like the Microsoft-vs-Linux moment of the 90s, ultimately doomed. Boundaries are threatened. Product managers are designing. Designers are coding. Engineers are doing product. A designer at Aboard, given a Claude Code task, popped out a full React Storybook component library in two hours.

The episode lands on a hard, honest non-answer. Rich asks: how do you price in the new world? Paul replies it's the wrong question. Asking "how do I keep the tax attorney worth $2,000" is a defense of the status quo that capitalism has never permitted. The right question is: how can I use the change in the world to drive value to the people who can most take advantage of it -- nonprofits, local press, mid-sized businesses, the under-served? Professionals will need to "self-commoditize" -- learn what business they're actually in (not just which discipline they're in), and partner with the AI tools rather than yelling at them. The hosts close with the Aboard pitch: a project that used to be four months and $500-600K with a team of five is now done in a month for a fraction of the cost. They're not hiding that value -- they're sharing it with their clients. Aboard Launch (30/60/90-day shipping) and Aboard Transform (talented teams embedded inside your org) are how they're operationalizing the new deal.

## Key Points

- **Paul confesses he doesn't know the future** ([1:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=83s)) -- Even running an AI firm, he stood up at Newsday and admitted nobody has the answers
- **Boundaries are blurring -- not roles disappearing** ([3:52](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=232s)) -- Designer, engineer, PM disciplines are bleeding into each other; the *boundaries* are what's threatened
- **The "asterisk" trap** ([4:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=280s)) -- Everyone in the AI space is hunting for the one thing the model "can never really do" -- and it's the wrong way to think
- **Goods vs services** ([6:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=360s)) -- Rich frames the labor market: a thing you sell (mint gum) vs an expertise you sell (law, radiology)
- **The expertise delta is the high-margin economy** ([7:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=451s)) -- White-collar margins exist because of the gap between what experts know and what their clients don't
- **Specialists' defensiveness is rational, not just protective** ([8:28](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=508s)) -- Lawyers and doctors warning "don't sign that, don't diagnose that" are doing their job; the tech really did land too fast
- **Doctors as the model** ([9:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=555s)) -- Medicine has 100+ years of protocols, so doctors *know* exactly where AI is allowed in (cuts down on bureaucracy) and where it isn't (diagnosis). Most other professions don't have that clarity
- **Product managers, designers, engineers as a three-legged stool** ([9:58](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=598s)) -- Always interdependent, always tense; AI is amplifying the tension
- **Skill simulators** ([10:37](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=637s)) -- Paul compares LLMs to flight simulators -- you still can't certify in one, but the line is blurring
- **Status-quo defense is human, not bad** ([15:07](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=907s)) -- "If I've got a pretty good spot in the status quo and a threat shows up..." you defend it. You have to pay for college tuition
- **SaaS flipped to "service as a software"** ([15:41](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=941s)) -- The Noah Brier (or maybe Paul) coinage for the era where service work is increasingly delivered as just-in-time software
- **Agentrification** ([16:34](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=994s)) -- Paul's portmanteau for an agency staffed by AI agents
- **LinkedIn is angry** ([17:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=1028s)) -- Paul's professional feed is filled with engineers and software people processing the AI moment with hot takes; people are pissed
- **The Linux-in-the-closet analogy** ([14:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=840s)) -- 90s Microsoft saw open-source web servers as existential. Now Linux runs inside Windows. Eventually it converges. But the moment was real
- **Storybook in two hours** ([18:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=1126s)) -- Designer, learning Claude Code, built a full React Storybook component library two hours after being given the task -- something designers traditionally never touched
- **The $2,000/hour tax lawyer** ([21:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=1260s)) -- Tax attorneys are the canonical case of expertise scarcity; ChatGPT now writes plain-English tax strategy in seconds
- **"How do you price?" is the wrong question** ([21:47](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=1307s)) -- It's a defense-of-the-old-world question. Capitalism never lets you ask "how do I stay valuable forever"
- **The right question** ([22:27](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=1347s)) -- "How can I use the change in the world to drive value to the people who can most take advantage of it?" -- nonprofits, local press, mid-sized business
- **Self-commoditize** ([22:44](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=1364s)) -- Tax attorneys should embrace the tools, validate clients' LLM chats as a free service, stay in the workflow
- **There may not be a perfect victory** ([26:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=_av0gfBqqrE&t=1573s)) -- "Multi-trillion dollar aircraft carriers coming into port" -- the scale of the change makes neat resolutions impossible
