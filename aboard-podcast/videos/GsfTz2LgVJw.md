# LevAIthan? A Hobbesian Look at AI

## Summary

Rich Ziade hands Paul Ford a 17th-century framing for the AI moment: Thomas Hobbes's *Leviathan*. Hobbes, writing amid the chaos of the English Civil War, argued that absolute individual freedom produces a life that is "solitary, poor, nasty, brutish, and short" — and that people must surrender some autonomy to a common authority to have any order at all. Rich's thesis: the AI industry is currently in a "pre-Hobbesian" state, a disruption phase where AI has so profoundly empowered the individual that collaboration and coordination have broken down. Everyone shows up to the meeting with their own private build, their own AI-generated requirements doc, their own eight-second demo, each convinced they'll stop the room in its tracks — while everyone else did the exact same thing.

The hosts, who run the software consultancy Aboard, describe living this in real time. Rich has been asking his team for weekly point releases and release notes for months and been "summarily ignored" because nobody wants to get out of the race car and back into a Ford Fiesta. Paul confesses he built a CRM alone and made "ten times too much software," realizing he should have gone on bike rides and thought about what he actually needed instead. They diagnose the results as slop: expertise marginalized, engineers designing with no taste, "the sepia-toned internet that is the product of Claude," and one-man bands all crowding into the orchestra pit until it "sounds like shit." AI, they argue, "punishes laziness" — anything made without full comprehension tends to be bad.

The way out isn't a monarch but a "pinch of process": speed bumps that force people to check in, respect for specialized disciplines like UX and product management, and humility about relinquishing some control. Paul reaches for William Morris's line that "you can't have art without resistance in the material" — creativity needs constraints, and slop is what happens when work slips out of the form an audience can perceive. Software used to carry the natural constraints of cost and difficulty; now that those are gone, people have to reimpose the discipline themselves. Their business pitch lands on exactly this: Aboard slows things down just a bit, puts great people on it, and gets them working together — which, it turns out, is what clients actually want.
