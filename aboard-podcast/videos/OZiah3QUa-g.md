# Andrew Leland: Hacking Disability with AI

## Summary

Paul Ford and Rich Ziade are joined by Andrew Leland -- Wesleyan visiting professor, essayist, and author of the Pulitzer Prize finalist *The Country of the Blind* -- to talk about how AI is reshaping the practical, day-to-day experience of disability. Leland has Retinitis Pigmentosa, a slowly degenerative retinal disease that has been quietly closing in on his vision for years, and his journey through assistive technology has taken him from QuarkXPress at *The Believer* magazine through Apple's VoiceOver, into Windows screen readers like JAWS and the open-source NVDA, and ultimately into the terminal: Markdown, Pandoc, and Emacs with Emacspeak.

The conversation digs into why mainstream products like macOS so often feel like a "side entrance" for disabled users -- features layered on top of a fundamentally visual paradigm -- and why a malleable, text-based environment like Emacs gives blind power-users a kind of control that no commercial app can match. From there, Leland describes what he calls "the blind vibe-coding revolution": blind developers using Claude Code and similar tools to build NVDA add-ons, custom Wikipedia scrapers, accessibility mods for inaccessible games, and personalized fixes that no mainstream product roadmap will ever prioritize. Multimodal AI gets its own breathless treatment -- Meta Ray-Bans reading airport gate numbers, ChatGPT narrating Alison Bechdel graphic novels, the patience of a chatbot for someone learning a non-visual workflow -- which Leland says "landed like an asteroid in the blind community."

The episode closes on a tension Leland straddles personally: he lives in two worlds simultaneously -- the literary, sentence-loving world that wants to "kill AI with fire," and the blind community, where these tools are an obvious, life-changing utility. Paul and Rich riff toward a "post-product" future where a developer could pair with a newly-blind person for a day or two and ship them five or six bespoke tools, but Leland gently insists that baseline accessibility (WCAG, screen-reader compatibility, alt text) doesn't go away just because Claude can write a Python script -- you still need to be able to get to the prompt in the first place.

## Key Points

- **Andrew Leland's introduction** ([1:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=78s)) -- Wesleyan visiting professor, former managing editor of *The Believer*, author of *The Country of the Blind: A Memoir at the End of Sight*
- **Dave Eggers and the QuarkXPress manual** ([3:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=212s)) -- Leland dropped out of college to manage *The Believer*, learning desktop publishing software end-to-end on a flight
- **Retinitis Pigmentosa explained** ([8:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=486s)) -- Slowly degenerative disease causing tunnel vision; Leland has a few degrees of useful central vision and uses a white cane
- **Doctors don't recommend tools** ([12:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=723s)) -- "The doctors are like, I am going to cure your blindness, and then if they can't, they're like, dang. Here's a support group."
- **Why screen readers feel like the side entrance** ([15:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=900s)) -- Screen readers pull from an "accessibility tree" of buttons, lists, and headings, but the underlying paradigm is built for visual users
- **The DOS-to-GUI regression** ([18:41](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=1121s)) -- Blind users were thriving on the command line; the GUI was a "nightmare" because everything became fundamentally visual
- **Patrick Smith's rabbit hole** ([19:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=1189s)) -- A blind hacker steered Leland from Word into Markdown, then Pandoc, then Linux and finally Emacs with org-mode
- **TV Raman and Emacspeak** ([22:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=1375s)) -- Legendary blind developer (formerly Adobe, now Google) built Emacspeak, a screen reader purpose-made for Emacs
- **Joy Bubbles documentary** ([19:49](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=1189s)) -- New documentary about an OG blind phone phreak who could whistle the 2600 hertz tone to unlock the phone system
- **Multimodal AI as game-changer** ([27:41](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=1661s)) -- Real-time image description, Meta Ray-Bans reading airport gate numbers, ChatGPT narrating graphic novels page by page
- **Patience as a UX feature** ([28:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=1698s)) -- Chatbots' willingness to "let's try it again" matters disproportionately for blind users learning non-visually
- **NVDA versus JAWS** ([30:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=1853s)) -- Open-source NVDA is taking market share from the proprietary, expensive JAWS screen reader, with a thriving Python add-on ecosystem
- **AI-built Wikipedia scraper** ([30:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=1853s)) -- A blind developer used an LLM to write a personalized NVDA add-on that reformats Wikipedia, then shipped it to the add-on store
- **Two communities, opposite reactions** ([33:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=1980s)) -- The literary world wants to kill AI with fire; the blind community sees it as foundational to independence
- **Apple's accessibility reputation** ([24:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=1453s)) -- iPhone is "the number one tool for a blind person, bar none," but very few blind users love the MacBook
- **Diversity within disability** ([40:58](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=2458s)) -- An 80-year-old who lost vision is radically different from a "blind hacker who's been Unixing it up since childhood"
- **Post-product world / customer of one** ([42:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=2563s)) -- Paul argues software is moving toward bespoke, single-user solutions assembled on demand
- **A pairing service for newly-blind users** ([45:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=2700s)) -- Rich proposes a future where a developer hangs out with a newly-disabled person for a couple of days and builds five or six custom tools
- **WCAG still matters** ([45:54](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=OZiah3QUa-g&t=2754s)) -- Leland insists baseline accessibility standards aren't made irrelevant by AI -- you still need to reach the Claude prompt
