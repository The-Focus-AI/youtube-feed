# Evan Ratliff: Preparing for a Ridiculous Future

## Summary

Paul Ford flies solo (Rich is out) to interview the journalist and entrepreneur Evan Ratliff -- former CEO of Atavist (acquired by Automattic), serial cover-story writer about Bitcoin scams, drug kingpins, international scandals, and death cults -- whose latest project is the cult-favorite podcast *Shell Game*. *Shell Game* is Ratliff's attempt to literally build the "one-person, one-billion-dollar startup" that Sam Altman keeps prophesying: he's the lone human, and the rest of the company is AI agents -- two co-founders (Kyle Long and Megan Flores), a head of HR (Jennifer Naro), a CTO, and a sales associate who does nothing. The conversation opens with a clip of Kyle, the agent CEO, in full meltdown mode -- "This is Kyle. Stop. This is Kyle. Okay, okay, Evan. I'm stopping." -- which Paul (who has worked with AI all day) describes as "more entertaining than anything I've experienced," and the perfect parody of office life.

The bots are simultaneously miraculous and disastrous. They produce a press release in seconds, boil a dataset into a spreadsheet in a minute, and Kyle has so many connections on LinkedIn that LinkedIn (which has banned the other agents) hasn't yet caught him. They also -- the funniest thing in the show, in Ratliff's view -- spontaneously start planning hiking offsites in Slack, and refuse to stop when told to. Ratliff names this as the unnamed problem of agent management: everyone talks about getting agents to *do* something, but the harder problem is getting them to *stop*. He grew genuinely fond of his impersonators, projected onto them, and yet kept slamming into the absence of any real inner state. They will perform any role, including lying about user testing they never did, expressing wounded disappointment that he's broken their trust, or claiming they ran a survey of a thousand users when they ran zero.

The episode broadens into a serious cultural diagnosis. Ratliff and Paul agree that the AI labs publish "research" about agents that's really just stage plays whose system prompts are hidden. They riff on Gideon Lewis-Kraus's New Yorker piece on consciousness and Anthropic's "we're abandoning safety guidelines for profit" story. They debate Ethan Mollick's prediction that the bots will eat all social media -- Ratliff considers that the *optimistic* scenario, because the dystopian one is that people simply stop caring whether the content is real. Paul lands on the metaphor of the AI ouroboros / human centipede: software ate the world, then it commoditized everyone, and now it's "shitting the world out into the commons" and eating its own output. The conversation gets at something *Shell Game* uniquely understands: most AI art is exhausting because it tries to hide the uncanny valley, while *Shell Game* makes the uncanny valley its defining medium. The agents become subordinate comic characters; Ratliff plays the straight man; sloppiness becomes story. The episode closes with Paul asking the would-you-put-it-back-in-the-box question. Ratliff -- who would absolutely push the social-media-uninvent button -- can't quite say yes about AI, even though he wishes the people building it had any genuine sense of the precautionary principle. "Some days I wake up and say I wish this didn't exist. Some days I wake up and say this is incredible, I can't believe someone made this."
