# Can AI be good? Josh Tyrangiel in conversation with Paul Ford

## Summary

Author and journalist Josh Tyrangiel joins Aboard's Paul Ford and Rich Ziade to discuss his new book *AI for Good*, a deliberate counter to the noisy polemics around AI. Rather than profiling the labs and billionaires, Tyrangiel went looking for the "downstream" people — teachers, doctors, and bureaucrats — who stumbled into AI to solve specific, tangible problems, often with no software background. The through-line is that there are no home runs in the book: progress comes from stubborn, sincere, non-technical people willing to grind through institutional friction, be the "jerk who says no," and fight software vendors until the tools actually fit the work.

Tyrangiel walks through a series of concrete case studies from the Cleveland Clinic — AI scribe software that saves doctors hours a week (though half the volunteers never used it), and sepsis prediction software that flags patients red/yellow/green and, once integrated into the workflow, cut in-hospital sepsis mortality by 41%. He recounts Palantir engineers building a working patient-flow prototype in three days, and tells the story of physicist Christie Jungst, who left her field to build AI-powered communication tools for nonverbal children after her son was diagnosed nonverbal — synthesizing a library of 7,000 sounds into 80,000 and drafting off zero-shot translation research to attack a problem "we didn't even know we wanted to solve."

Looking ahead, Tyrangiel frames his optimism around an unlikely source: public anger. After 25 years of passive acceptance of technology, people booing Eric Schmidt at graduation speeches signal a healthier, less passive democracy that is finally forcing companies and government to reckon with consequences. He argues the labs are a "very different kind of intelligence" trapped in a bubble who simply aren't thinking about what good AI should do — so it falls to everyone else to deploy it well. He closes on education, where he sees the real leverage in empowering teachers to escape "teaching to the middle" and personalize learning, and offers the everyday public a way in: photograph your dying houseplant, ask an AI what to do.

## Key Points

- **The book is about people, not tech companies** ([2:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=162s)) - A counter to Karen Hao's *Empire*; focused on the underdogs downstream trying to use AI for COVID response, disability communication, and other neglected use cases
- **The Levine provocation** ([4:07](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=247s)) - Paul reads NYC Comptroller Mark Levine's boilerplate AI paragraph to ask how genuine civic good actually gets started versus "big AI thoughts and not enough action"
- **Progress depends on stubborn humans** ([6:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=392s)) - Success comes from the handful willing to be "the jerk who says no" to both colleagues and software vendors
- **Most subjects are not technologists** ([7:38](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=458s)) - They often had no idea what AI did; they just knew they had a data problem
- **Danny Hillis's advice** ([8:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=493s)) - "Stop talking to the tech companies. Go find the tech in the world without the tech companies" — the origin of the book's approach
- **No revolution overnight** ([12:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=733s)) - The Cleveland Clinic, one of the best hospitals in the world, grinds out a 2.1% profit; change is evolutionary, not instant
- **AI scribe software** ([13:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=811s)) - Phone app records the visit, fills electronic health records, sends labs, translates; saves hours a week, but half the volunteering doctors never used it
- **Sepsis prediction** ([15:02](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=902s)) - Sepsis kills 350,000 Americans a year; Bayesian software flags patients red/yellow/green without making the final call
- **41% mortality reduction** ([16:33](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=993s)) - Over a year, doctors and technologists integrated the software into the workflow, cutting in-hospital sepsis mortality by 41%
- **Some people never change** ([18:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=1080s)) - Cardiologists shown a faster, better technique often refuse; realism about how fast the world will change
- **Doctors have discipline control** ([19:05](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=1145s)) - Paul's hypothesis: regulated professions can refuse policy, so AI can't be thrust on them — unlike do-gooder orgs that get scared off by bad news
- **Don't hate the game** ([21:30](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=1290s)) - On Palantir: hate the players if you must, but the game is good; Ukraine has "no war without iPads and Palantir"
- **Hospitals are hotels with no data** ([22:22](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=1342s)) - Unlike hotels, hospitals don't know when patients arrive or leave; Palantir built a working patient-flow prototype in three days
- **The labs aren't thinking about good** ([25:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=1503s)) - Researchers are a different intelligence in a competitive bubble; deploying AT for societal benefit will be on the rest of us
- **Nick Clegg on democracies** ([27:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=1641s)) - AI exposes a weak spot for large democracies "incapable of having mature conversations"; the US sits uncomfortably in the middle
- **Optimism from anger** ([31:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=1863s)) - Booing Eric Schmidt is a sign people are finally active, not passive, about the technology being handed to them
- **Quiet regulation returns** ([33:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=1980s)) - After AI czar David Sacks left in March, the Trump administration signaled it might start regulating models over a certain size
- **Christie Jungst's story** ([35:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=2143s)) - A physicist who left her field to build communication tools for nonverbal children after her son's diagnosis
- **Synthesizing sound data** ([39:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=2340s)) - A library of 7,000 sounds became 80,000 via AI synthesis; drafting off zero-shot translation research; $79M in funding from Yale and others
- **Journalism has a moat** ([43:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=2591s)) - AI replicates the past, but good journalists tell people new things — a natural defense if handled carefully
- **Scribe tools aren't commodities** ([45:14](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=2714s)) - Of five AI scribe tools, three required too much editing; one had two-minute review time and high accuracy
- **Teachers, not students** ([51:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=3102s)) - The education leverage is with teachers who turn lessons into labs; one 30-year veteran rebuilt her plan with a Khan Academy bot
- **The middle is imaginary** ([52:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=GHQM81L7ALI&t=3162s)) - AI lets teachers escape "teaching to the middle" and personalize instruction across a class of 25
