# The AI Consulting Paradox

## Summary

Paul Ford and Rich Ziade open with a field report from a visit by a New School journalism class that came through the Aboard offices: roughly fourteen students, ages 19-21, who treat AI not as a revolution but as a utility "over in the corner." Several said outright that they don't want to use it too much in school because they're "supposed to be learning how to think." Paul reads this as a healthy break from the social-media playbook -- AI doesn't pull on people the way Facebook or Twitter do, because the endpoints aren't other humans. Rich agrees and goes a step further: "AI gets boring." Wikipedia is weird because it's an artifact of humans negotiating; LLM output is just digested.

The bulk of the episode then dissects an article from *The Guardian* in which PwC US CEO Paul Griggs warns his 365,000 employees, in roughly so many words, that anyone who isn't "AI first" or "paranoid" about the technology won't be at the firm long. The hosts use this to frame what they call the "AI consulting paradox": at the individual-contributor level, the message is simple -- use the tools, you'll be more productive. But at the partner level, where someone is bringing in $15 million a year billing PwC people against legacy ERP integrations, PDF mills, and offshore teams, AI is essentially a tool to take that $15 million figure down to $5 million. So managers and partners drag their feet, dress up legacy work in AI clothing, and signal compliance without actually cannibalizing themselves -- much as Netflix had to do during its rough transition from mail-in DVDs to streaming.

In the back half, Paul and Rich design what a "consulting firm for the new era" should look like: strategy-heavy at the front, where AI is genuinely bad (it can't sit in a boardroom and interview your users), and AI-native on the back end where execution is now nearly free. Big Four firms have to compete with Accenture certifying 30,000 people on Claude Code and with private equity buyers who don't care about Stan's twenty-year contract -- they bought you to make you efficient. The episode closes on the human reality of professional services: spouses come out for dinner together, partners have earned trust over twenty years, and that "rainbow-colored tornado" of AI transformation will reach those steakhouse relationships, but it'll take time.
