# I Tried To Make Something In America.

## Summary

Michael Kristy of The Iron Snail recounts a near-catastrophic attempt to manufacture high-quality wool sweaters in the United States. After sending 1,000 pounds of raw wool to a domestic spinner in May 2024, the resulting yarn was rejected by two knitting partners — it was torquing, snapping, and taking 9-10 times longer than normal to work with. When Michael requested a refund, the spinner ("Mr. Buck Wild") fired back a ferociously angry midnight email mocking the knitters' analysis and telling Michael he needed to pivot as a businessman.

The personal crisis becomes a broader meditation on the decline of U.S. textile manufacturing. While Japan and Italy invested in luxury technology and government-backed supply chains, the U.S. industry — once protected by tariffs — got smashed at both the low-cost and luxury ends when those walls came down. Today, surviving American manufacturers largely depend on Department of Defense contracts via the Berry Amendment, leaving small consumer brands like Iron Snail to absorb all the supply-chain risk themselves: paying 50% deposits up front, then waiting 6-18 months for delivery while their cash sits locked up.

To stop the bleeding, Michael brought in supply-chain expert Courtney and launched "Operation Gumby," a four-part survival plan: fill manufacturing gaps with international partners when domestic ones fail, slash domestic orders by 75% but place them quarterly for consistency, send all materials to NC State's Wilson College of Textiles for R&D, and eventually build a "hub" that runs ready-made fabrics to stabilize manufacturers. The yarn was ultimately rescued by Japanese spinners who applied the two simple finishing treatments Mr. Buck Wild had skipped — and the Buckwild Beanie was born.
