# The 125-Year-Old Wool Jacket Brands Still Can't Beat.

## Summary

Michael Kristy dives into the history and textile science behind Brown's Beach Cloth, a legendary outdoor fabric introduced by William W. Brown in 1901. Originally made for lumberjacks and outdoorsmen in Connecticut and later Worcester, Massachusetts, the fabric became so popular that it was worn by delivery boys, milkmen, and even US Antarctic expeditions. The central irony is that this "space age" fabric's secret ingredient was "shoddy" -- recycled wool fibers that had been despised in America since the Civil War, when unscrupulous manufacturers used cheap recycled materials to produce uniforms that fell apart in the rain.

What made Brown's Beach Cloth revolutionary was its warp-knit construction, a technique that sits between traditional weaving and knitting. Unlike weft knits (t-shirts, sweaters) which can unravel and sag, warp knits won't ladder if punctured and maintain structural stability, yet they retain the comfort, flexibility, and loft of knitted fabric. The outer face uses a two-ply wool-cotton yarn for strength and abrasion resistance, while the inner fleece layer is made from the recycled "shoddy" wool -- which actually works perfectly for fleece because the fiber lengths don't matter when you're intentionally destroying the yarn structure to create loft.

The video concludes that Brown's design compromises -- woven binding edges to prevent sagging, snap buttons instead of traditional buttonholes, and rounded shapes for better fabric flow -- actually became beloved design features. The vest, in particular, became Brown's most important product because it let workers "test drive" the new fabric without replacing their entire kit. Though synthetic fibers killed the company in the 1960s, Michael argues that Brown was a visionary who foresaw the shift from woven to knit workwear that is fully underway in 2026.
