# Eric Ries: How AI Disrupts Capitalism and What Comes Next

## Summary

Eric Ries — the author of The Lean Startup, who about 15 years ago made the MVP and learning-by-experiment vocabulary that every builder now takes for granted — joins Ksenia to talk about his new book Incorruptible, which he frames as "the missing manual, the second half of what I wish someone had told me." The core argument: the more successful a company becomes, the more valuable it is as a target. The more golden the goose, the greater the temptation to butcher it. Most builders, Eric included, were naive about what happens next. They get surrounded by people who know how to make money without creating value, and they're expected to defer to them. Corruption in business, in his definition, is what our great-grandparents would have called this exact pattern: making money while no longer creating value. It shows up in flavors (food after PE takeover), in board meetings ("what's the ROI?" when the highest-leverage decisions look like negative-ROI on a spreadsheet), and in the silent collapse of an organization's ethos.

The core conceptual move of the book is from policy/strategy/org-chart language into a physics-of-organizations frame. Organizations live in financial gravity — a hostile environment that pulls them toward mediocrity through convergent evolution. The first two-thirds of the book is how to see, understand, and structurally resist that gravity. The last third, "To Bend the Light," is the more ambitious claim: successful organizations become gravitational actors themselves and can pull the economy around them. Trust and love are not soft language — they are, in Eric's framing, the electromagnetic force of organizations, small in scale but vastly stronger than gravity at the human scale. Brian Chesky (during Airbnb's pandemic-zero moment) told him "at the end of the day, businesses are built on love and trust." When a customer truly bonds with you, they're no longer eligible to be part of your competitor's TAM. That's the most ruthless business strategy of all.

The conversation goes broader than business. Eric reads our moment as a battle for the soul of the economy and civic infrastructure, with mass institutional collapse — newspapers, parties, sports leagues, universities, big law, retailers, hotels. AI sweeping through is going to accelerate the chaos, especially because the regulating institutions that would normally absorb the shock are being dismantled. But that creates a builder-shaped hole: every collapsed institution has to be replaced, and the values encoded in the replacement are up for grabs. The bad version of AI is the cost-cutting-driven layoff frenzy currently being rewarded with rising stock prices ("really gross"). The good version is AI as a "mission guardian" — using LLMs' superpower (summarization) to make organizational reality legible to leaders for the first time, verify that operating procedures align with stated mission, and build coherence and trust. He defines AGI by noting we already passed the 10-year-old definition; the goalposts just keep moving. His book recommendation is The Enlightened Capitalists by James O'Toole — 200 years of entrepreneurs trying to build a more enlightened capitalism and getting destroyed by the market.
