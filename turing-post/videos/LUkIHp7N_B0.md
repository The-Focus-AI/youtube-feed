# OpenAI's Michael Bolin: What Engineers Still Matter For in the Age of Coding Agents

## Summary

In this second part of an interview with Michael Bolin, tech lead for open-source Codex at OpenAI, host Ksenia Se shifts the conversation from harness engineering to the human side of the AI coding revolution. Bolin reflects on what it means to be a programmer when agents write most of the code, arguing that the engineer's identity was never really about the physical typing but about shaping the final artifact. He notes that prototypes have become dramatically cheaper to build, making imagination rather than time the primary bottleneck in software development.

Bolin identifies "engineering taste" as the critical skill for the AI era -- the ability to read code, recognize what could be better, and ask the right questions at the right level of abstraction. He candidly acknowledges that agents will take over writing most code, and that even he rarely types code anymore except for security-sensitive work. His main concern is a failure mode where developers abdicate their taste too early, producing bloated or fragile systems because no human applied architectural judgment along the way.

The conversation closes with Bolin's book recommendation -- Neal Stephenson's Cryptonomicon -- which he connects to his love of creating systems and being his own adversary. Throughout, Bolin strikes a balance between genuine excitement about agent-driven development and a measured insistence that human oversight remains essential, at least for now.

## Key Points

- **Switching mindset to build for agents** ([0:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LUkIHp7N_B0&t=42s)) - Bolin says building tools for agents instead of developers is "a bit different but not wildly different" since agents are essentially one customer with consistent opinions
- **Agents consuming the web** ([1:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LUkIHp7N_B0&t=66s)) - Bolin wonders what the web looks like when agents consume more of it than humans, calling it a story "not yet written"
- **Programmer identity crisis** ([1:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LUkIHp7N_B0&t=86s)) - Despite identifying strongly with writing code for 20 years, Bolin says he realized it was always about the artifact, not the typing
- **Building more than ever** ([2:16](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LUkIHp7N_B0&t=136s)) - Prototypes that would have taken an entire weekend are now cheap to build, removing time as the limiting factor
- **Knowing the right question to ask** ([3:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LUkIHp7N_B0&t=180s)) - Key engineering skills now include reading code with taste, navigating abstraction levels, and being inquisitive
- **Agents will take over coding** ([4:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LUkIHp7N_B0&t=253s)) - Bolin confirms agents will write most code; he already rarely types code himself except for security-sensitive work
- **Engineering taste as last human advantage** ([4:57](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LUkIHp7N_B0&t=297s)) - Bolin says it will be "interesting when I've decided that Codex unilaterally has better taste than me" and assumes they will get there eventually
- **Imagination as the new bottleneck** ([5:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LUkIHp7N_B0&t=318s)) - Time was historically the limiting factor; now it is imagination, with lower barriers to entry for trying new stacks
- **Risk of abdicating taste too early** ([6:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LUkIHp7N_B0&t=371s)) - The failure mode is giving agents too much credit too early, resulting in bloated systems nobody applied judgment to
- **More than just spouting requirements** ([6:52](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LUkIHp7N_B0&t=412s)) - Random ping-ponging of requirements at a machine will not produce the best artifact; you need architectural opinion
- **Agents self-reviewing architecture** ([7:28](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LUkIHp7N_B0&t=448s)) - The Codex team already asks agents nightly to review architectural decisions; the harness will eventually learn to do this automatically
- **Cryptonomicon as formative book** ([8:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=LUkIHp7N_B0&t=497s)) - Neal Stephenson's novel shaped Bolin's love of creating systems and being his own adversary
