# Vikings, Ragnar, Berserkers, Valhalla & the Warriors of the Viking Age | Lex Fridman Podcast #495

## Summary

Historian Lars Brownworth -- author of *The Sea Wolves* and *The Normans: From Raiders to Kings*, and the host of the pioneering *12 Byzantine Rulers* and *Norman Centuries* podcasts -- joins Lex Fridman for a sweeping two-hour tour of the Viking Age (793-1066 AD). The conversation begins with the psychologically shattering raid on the holy island of Lindisfarne in 793, where Norse warriors broke a centuries-old social contract that had made the sea a barrier and monastic islands sacred sanctuaries. Brownworth argues that this single raid announced an entirely new model of warfare: technologically sophisticated, terrifyingly fast, and unconstrained by Christian rules of sanctuary.

The dialogue traces how a unique combination of clinker-built longships (capable of crossing the North Atlantic yet drawing only two feet of water for river travel), brutal pragmatism, and a fatalistic Norse worldview centered on Ragnarok produced one of history's most effective raiding cultures. Brownworth walks through the legend of Ragnar Lothbrok and his sons leading the Great Heathen Army to invade England, the founding of Normandy by Hrolf the Walker (Rollo), the Viking explorations west to Iceland, Greenland, and Leif Erikson's Vinland five centuries before Columbus, and the Swedish Varangians sailing down the Russian river systems to found the Kievan Rus' and serve as the elite Varangian Guard of the Byzantine Emperor.

A recurring theme is "creative destruction" -- the way Vikings destroyed Charlemagne's bloated Carolingian world only to clear the ground for stronger states (England under Alfred the Great, France under the Capetians, the Norman kingdoms of Sicily and England). Brownworth ends with reflections on the Byzantine Empire as the buffer that protected Europe from Islamic conquest, the lessons of states that lasted a thousand years, and the great-man theory of history. The conversation closes with the Viking spirit captured in Tennyson's *Ulysses* -- "to strive, to seek, to find, and not to yield."

## Key Points

- **The Viking Age opens with Lindisfarne, June 793** ([3:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=203s)) - A small Norse force, probably from Norway, slaughtered the monks of the holy island of Lindisfarne, signaling that the sea was no longer a barrier and that sacred monastic communities were no longer safe
- **Alcuin's letter captures the psychic shock** ([3:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=235s)) - Charlemagne's favorite scholar wrote that "never before has such terror appeared in Britain... nor was it thought that such an inroad from the sea could be made," underscoring how unimaginable the raid was
- **Norse runes were used for spells, not literature** ([7:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=463s)) - The Vikings had a written alphabet primarily used for naming swords and casting curses, so almost everything we know about them was written by their victims, leaving us with a deeply biased record
- **A sword in the crib** ([9:28](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=568s)) - A Swedish Viking father reportedly placed a sword in his newborn son's crib saying "may you have nothing in this life but what you can gain with this," capturing the harshness of Viking child-rearing
- **The longship is the Vikings' great secret** ([12:58](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=778s)) - Clinker-built ships could cross the Atlantic yet drew less than two feet of water; 20 men could portage one around an obstruction, allowing strikes hundreds of miles inland up shallow rivers
- **Speed was the most frightening Viking weapon** ([14:18](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=858s)) - English foot armies moved 10-15 miles a day, cavalry 20, but Viking longships could average 70-120 miles a day, making it nearly impossible to assemble defenders in time
- **Vikings used terror as deliberate strategy** ([16:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=991s)) - They scouted as traders to learn local schedules, then attacked on Easter, Christmas, and high holy days when wealth was concentrated and defenders distracted
- **Aethelred the Unready paid 7.5 million silver pennies** ([24:29](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=1469s)) - In a single year the English king paid 48,000 pounds of silver -- equivalent to 50 adult elephants in weight -- to make the Vikings go away, which only attracted more raiders
- **Ragnar Lothbrok's hairy breeches** ([26:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=1571s)) - The legendary Viking, possibly a composite figure, was supposedly thrown into a snake pit by King Aelle of Northumbria but was protected by his magical "hairy breeches" until they were stripped off
- **The blood eagle revenge** ([30:36](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=1836s)) - Ivar the Boneless allegedly performed the first "blood eagle" on King Aelle, cutting open the back, removing the lungs, and laying them on the back so they fluttered like wings as the victim breathed
- **The Frankish kings named by hatred** ([27:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=1620s)) - Lars notes the Frankish royal succession can be read as a quality scorecard: Charles the Great, Louis the Pious, Charles the Fat, Charles the Bald, Charles the Simple
- **"We are all kings"** ([36:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=2160s)) - When asked who their king was at the 845 siege of Paris, the Vikings replied "we have no king, we are all kings" -- a flat meritocracy where leaders were judged solely by their ability to win and distribute treasure
- **Rollo refused to kiss the king's foot** ([43:10](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=2590s)) - At the 911 Treaty of Saint-Clair-sur-Epte, Rollo had a guard pick up Charles the Simple's foot to his mouth, knocking the king onto his back -- a perfect symbol of the Norman-French relationship for centuries
- **Vikings as "creative destruction"** ([48:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=2880s)) - By destroying the Carolingian status quo, the Vikings cleared the ground for stronger European states: Alfred the Great's Wessex became England, France emerged from Charlemagne's wreckage, and the Normans conquered Sicily and England
- **Valhalla as practice for losing** ([54:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=3240s)) - The Viking afterlife was where dead warriors fought and died every day to be resurrected the next morning -- training for Ragnarok, the final battle they were destined to lose
- **Berserkers as Odin's chosen warriors** ([53:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=3203s)) - Odin's elite warriors would attack with teeth and nails, ignore severed limbs, even attack other Vikings -- where the modern word "berserk" comes from
- **Hospitality as survival technology** ([57:04](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=3424s)) - Norse religion taught that Odin traveled in disguise testing households, blessing the hospitable and murdering the inhospitable, a ritualization of the survival imperative in a brutal northern winter
- **Greenland as the greatest real estate scam** ([1:10:44](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=4244s)) - Eric the Red called the icy island "Greenland" specifically to attract land-hungry Icelanders, then took 25 ships of which only 14 made the crossing
- **Leif Erikson's Vinland abandoned after 3 years** ([1:14:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=4451s)) - The Norse settlement at L'Anse aux Meadows in Newfoundland failed because the Vikings refused to abandon husbandry, were 2,000 miles from resupply, and faced relentless Algonquin (whom they called "Skraelings") attacks
- **The Varangian Guard at Hagia Sophia** ([1:22:07](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=4927s)) - Viking warriors served as the personal bodyguard of the Byzantine Emperor and famously carved Norse runes into the marble balcony of the Hagia Sophia during a long ceremony they didn't understand
- **Greek fire as Byzantine napalm** ([1:24:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=5063s)) - The Byzantines repelled Viking attacks on Constantinople with a flamethrower-like substance that floated and burned on water, so closely guarded that its formula remains unknown today
- **Cnut commands the waves** ([1:32:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=5552s)) - The North Sea Empire builder demonstrated humility by ordering the tide to retreat, then using its disobedience to silence courtiers who flattered him as all-powerful
- **The Byzantine collapse begins in 1025** ([1:43:57](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=5037s)) - After Basil II's death, the bureaucracy convinced itself it could run the empire without strong rulers, leading to the catastrophic 1071 Battle of Manzikert and the loss of Anatolia to the Turks
- **Vikings groomed daily and bathed weekly** ([1:51:35](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=6695s)) - English chroniclers complained Vikings were "soft" because they bathed too much and that English women found them too attractive due to their grooming -- the opposite of the dirty barbarian stereotype
- **Great-man theory holds** ([1:48:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=6501s)) - Brownworth argues you cannot swap out Martin Luther, Augustus, or Constantine and get the same history; impersonal forces are insufficient because everything is relational and human
- **The Frederick Douglass test** ([1:59:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=iKx3gAODybU&t=7153s)) - Douglass's line that "I could sit with Plato and Cicero and they would not flinch" captures Brownworth's belief that the great conversation is for everyone and that human nature is unchanging across millennia
