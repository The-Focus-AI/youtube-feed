# The Rise and Fall of the Roman Empire and the Byzantine Empire | Lex Fridman Podcast #498

## Summary

Anthony Kaldellis, historian of the Roman Empire and author of the thousand-page *The New Roman Empire*, joins Lex Fridman for a nearly four-hour tour through 2,200 years of Roman history, with the spotlight firmly on the Eastern Roman (Byzantine) Empire. Kaldellis frames Rome as a "ship of Theseus": every component -- language (Latin to Greek), religion (pagan to Orthodox Christian), geography (Rome to Constantinople) -- was gradually replaced, yet the political community never experienced a rupture and always understood itself as Roman. The recurring argument is that Rome was not defined by an emperor or a dynasty but by a *res publica*, a monarchical republic in which the emperor was expected to serve the Roman people.

The conversation moves chronologically through the hinges of that history: Diocletian's third-century reset that built the tax system and bureaucracy, the Edict of Caracalla (212 AD) that made every free inhabitant a citizen "and meant it," Constantine's founding of Constantinople and adoption of Christianity, Justinian's law code, wars of reconquest, and the Nika massacre, Heraclius and the ruinous Persian war, the Arab conquests, Greek fire, the failed sieges of Constantinople, iconoclasm, the Macedonian revival, and the slow terminal decline that Kaldellis dates to the 1340s. Throughout, he insists that taxation is "the heart of it," that emperors governed under a "perpetual referendum" (roughly 46% were violently overthrown), and that the army was almost never used as an instrument of social control.

Kaldellis's central revisionist claim is that the empire's crises were exogenous shocks -- swift, catastrophic foreign invasions -- while its defining trait was endogenous resilience: centuries-long stretches of quiet regrowth and self-healing that historians tend to skip over in their rush from peak to peak. Absent the invasions, he argues, this "monarchical republic" could have lasted another thousand years, because it never produced separatist movements, warlord partitions, or peasant uprisings. The episode closes on lessons for the modern "American empire": build institutions that work for the majority and explain to people why they cost what they cost, and narrow the gap between rhetoric and action.

## Key Points

- **The late Roman Empire = tax system + bureaucracy** ([6:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=363s)) - Kaldellis defines the era (Diocletian to the early 7th century) by its administration, the reforms of Diocletian and Constantine.
- **Three swift, catastrophic territorial losses** ([10:14](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=614s)) - The Arab conquests (630s), the Seljuk conquest of Asia Minor (1070s), and the Fourth Crusade (1204). Defeats were swift; most of the history is slow, steady growth.
- **Rome as a ship of Theseus** ([14:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=846s)) - Every component changed -- language, religion, geography -- yet the political community remained continuously Roman.
- **Consensus to be ruled, not just pride** ([18:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=1086s)) - For most subjects it was a mutual understanding: be taxed and ruled in exchange for what you get back. Kaldellis tests this by whether people paid taxes and stayed, not by elite expressions of pride.
- **Continuity you could test across centuries** ([24:43](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=1483s)) - A soldier's oath or a woman's business rights under Roman law would have been recognizable to Romans from any century -- concrete threads of a shared polity.
- **The imperial persona** ([27:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=1675s)) - Emperors broadcast a consistent image: responsive, accountable, proactive, tirelessly hard-working -- exercising public power solely for their subjects' benefit.
- **Judge by action, not rhetoric** ([30:58](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=1858s)) - Kaldellis's Machiavelli lesson -- and his finding that East Roman emperors were generally sincere: they mostly did what they said.
- **No right to the throne** ([32:52](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=1972s)) - Because the emperor emerged from a republic rather than founding a dynasty, no one had an inherent claim -- the structural source of imperial accountability.
- **Civil war as a feature, not a bug** ([36:22](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=2182s)) - About 120 civil wars over the thousand years Kaldellis studies; roughly 46% of emperors violently overthrown. The threat kept emperors responsive.
- **The Hippodrome and the perpetual referendum** ([42:50](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=2570s)) - Public assembly functioned as an ongoing opinion poll. Alexios III proposed a 'German tax,' the Hippodrome erupted, and he instantly backtracked.
- **The Edict of Caracalla (212 AD)** ([48:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=2911s)) - Extended citizenship to nearly all free inhabitants -- an explosion, not just an expansion. Within a generation the emperors were all provincials.
- **The Crisis of the Third Century** ([57:33](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=3453s)) - 26 emperors murdered in 50 years, hyperinflation, plague, and the empire briefly fracturing into three -- before Aurelian reunited it.
- **Diocletian's reset** ([1:04:42](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=3882s)) - Bigger government, the Tetrarchy, universal census and taxation (even Italy taxed for the first time) -- the model that endured for centuries.
- **Taxation is the heart of it** ([1:09:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=4148s)) - Kaldellis's single-factor answer for how the society held together and functioned.
- **Constantine founds Constantinople (330 AD)** ([1:16:09](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=4569s)) - A deliberate copy-and-paste of Rome in the East -- 'New Rome' -- built at the strategic Bosphorus crossing where Europe meets Asia.
- **Why Constantinople's geography mattered** ([1:18:54](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=4734s)) - A clamp midway between the Danube and Euphrates frontiers, letting emperors shift between European and Asian provinces. The empire never split there again.
- **Constantine as #1 emperor -- and a murderer** ([1:25:29](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=5129s)) - Ranked first for world-historical decisions (the city, Christianity), despite murdering his son Crispus and his wife for reasons he never even tried to justify.
- **Did Christianity triumph over Rome, or Rome capture Christianity?** ([1:28:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=5283s)) - Kaldellis leans to the latter: unlike Islam, Christianity arrived inside a fully developed state and was co-opted into the imperial system.
- **A half-millennium conversion** ([1:37:39](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=5859s)) - Christianity spread slowly via incentives (charity, church endowments) and disincentives (bans on sacrifice, restrictions on office) -- solidly Christian only by the 6th century.
- **A laboratory of Roman, Christian, and Greek identity** ([1:48:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=6535s)) - The only society that was all three at once, letting people go all-in on any of the elements or blend them.
- **The fall of the West as a vicious cycle** ([1:57:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=7066s)) - Lost provinces meant lost tax revenue, meaning fewer armies to stop the next barbarian wave. Geography funneled invaders west and spared the East.
- **The center shifted East, it did not collapse** ([2:03:38](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=7418s)) - By the 430s Constantinople was codifying Roman law for the West; under Justinian the East reconquered Rome. Roman law as we know it comes from Constantinople.
- **How the government worked: civilian and military** ([2:05:38](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=7538s)) - The army was the single largest expense (100,000-250,000 men); the civilian administration existed largely to fund it via census and taxation.
- **Taxation by exemption, not by code** ([2:10:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=7832s)) - There was no tax code -- people petitioned for exemptions, officials granted too many, and emperors periodically cancelled them all to start over.
- **Eunuchs as a parallel track of power** ([2:12:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=7931s)) - Loyal because they were outsiders with no dynasty to build, emperors used eunuchs -- even as generals, like Narses -- to counterbalance entrenched networks.
- **No isolated communities** ([2:17:12](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=8232s)) - Every arable plot was censused; church, calendar, and coinage enmeshed even remote villages in a dense institutional matrix.
- **The monarchical republic** ([2:25:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=8746s)) - A res publica -- a polity meant to serve the Roman people -- governed by a monarch. Not an 'empire' in the conquest sense, and no Greek word for it.
- **Why it was not a military dictatorship** ([2:27:12](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=8832s)) - Despite army-driven successions, the army was almost never used to suppress the population -- a dangerous tool that can backfire, as in the Arab Spring.
- **Justinian: peasant to emperor** ([2:30:44](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=9044s)) - Rose through his uncle Justin, married the former sex worker Theodora (changing the law to do so), codified Roman law, and reconquered western provinces.
- **Justinian's mixed legacy and the Nika massacre** ([2:42:44](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=9764s)) - Overextended the empire and stripped the East of armies; ordered 30,000+ protesters killed in the Nika revolt, then rebuilt Constantinople and Hagia Sophia.
- **Heraclius and the Persian war** ([2:47:54](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=10074s)) - A usurper whose civil war handed gains to Persia, who then defeated the Persians in spectacular Mesopotamian campaigns -- only to lose everything to the Arabs.
- **Surviving the Arab conquests** ([2:57:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=10652s)) - Losing Egypt cut Constantinople's grain; recovery took decades of holding a line, investment in fleets, and Greek fire to defeat sieges.
- **The second Arab siege fails: Leo III** ([3:01:32](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=10892s)) - A capable usurper with Bulgar allies and well-deployed Greek fire wore the Arabs down -- Kaldellis sees little role for luck.
- **Iconoclasm was socially overblown** ([3:05:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=11103s)) - Kaldellis found no popular interest -- maybe 100-200 clergy and emperors cared; the story survives only because they wrote the sources.
- **The 11th-century triple whammy** ([3:09:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=11361s)) - Normans in the west, Pechenegs from the north, and Seljuk Turks from the east -- external shocks, not internal moral decay, buckled the empire.
- **Exogenous shocks vs endogenous stability** ([3:15:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=11700s)) - Kaldellis's thesis: define the society by its centuries of self-healing regrowth, not by the brief foreign-driven crises.
- **When decline became irreversible** ([3:21:44](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=12104s)) - The 1340s -- loss of Asia Minor, civil wars, Serbian expansion, and the Black Death. It took the Turks another century, delayed by Tamerlane.
- **Absent invasions, another thousand years** ([3:25:14](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=12314s)) - No separatist movements (except Bulgaria), no loss of taxing power, no warlord partitions -- a remarkably self-healing system in the most dangerous neighborhood on earth.
- **Why it held together: two-part answer** ([3:28:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=12533s)) - Authorities genuinely persuaded subjects they ruled on their behalf, and a tightly unified Roman-Orthodox identity made the alternatives worse.
- **Lessons for the American 'empire'** ([3:34:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=12871s)) - Build institutions that work for the majority and explain why they cost what they cost -- and narrow the gap between rhetoric and action.
- **Human nature persists** ([3:40:46](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=13246s)) - With Thucydides against the postmodernists: culture sets the dials, but the same range of human types appears in any group across the centuries.
- **Three phases of human history** ([3:43:55](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=13435s)) - Hunter-gatherer, agricultural, and now industrial/technological -- with a likely fourth phase in which humans modify themselves.

- **Constantinople / New Rome / Byzantion** ([1:16:09](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=4569s)) - Constantine's eastern capital at the Bosphorus, grown from ~25,000 to 500,000 in two centuries via immigration.
- **The Hippodrome** ([42:50](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=2570s)) - Constantinople's stadium and political forum -- chariot races, acclamations, and popular consultation of the emperor.
- **Hagia Sophia** ([2:42:44](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=pv1TUJSEM2k&t=9764s)) - The magnificent church Justinian rebuilt after the Nika revolt; also a place of political assembly.
