# Will OpenAI Tank OpenClaw? | E2251

## Summary

This transcript from a February 2026 episode of *This Week in Startups* centers on the breaking news of OpenAI’s acquisition of the open-source sensation **OpenClaw**. Hosts Jason Calacanis and Alex Wilhelm discuss the strategic implications of the deal, which is estimated to be worth mid-to-high nine figures. While some view the acquisition as a "rug pull" of a promising open-source project, the discussion weighs two perspectives: a cynical view that Sam Altman is neutralizing an interface that threatened to commoditize OpenAI, and an optimistic view that the project’s move to a foundation led by Dave Morin will provide it with necessary resources. Calacanis emphasizes the need for a "counter-strategy," pledging to fund startups that focus on keeping OpenClaw's security, hosting, and skills open and decentralized.

The conversation shifts to the practical revolution triggered by OpenClaw, featuring guests Hiten Shah and Jesse Genet, who demonstrate how AI agents—or "replicants"—are replacing traditional software and user interfaces. Jesse Genet showcases how she used OpenClaw to build a custom, "slop-free" YouTube app for her children and an automated homeschooling curriculum, leading Calacanis to offer her an immediate $125,000 investment. Hiten Shah demonstrates how he built a personal CRM in minutes and maintains autonomous dashboards. A major theme of this segment is the "death of the UI," as founders move toward direct, text- or voice-based interactions with agents that possess the "agency" to perform complex professional tasks.

The final segment explores the ethical and legal frontier of **"digital immortality"** through a project created by John Arrow: an AI clone of the late cartoonist Scott Adams. The project sparked significant controversy after being banned by YouTube for potential viewer confusion. The discussion explores the "Right to Publicity" and the moral tension between a creator’s public wish to be memorialized via AI and their estate’s desire for privacy. The hosts and guests debate the need for "Do Not Replicate" (DNR) legal filings and how the falling cost of AI will lead to more frequent, unauthorized recreations of public figures, ultimately concluding that "break bread" negotiations with families are essential for such high-fidelity digital replicas.
