# The project SpaceX's first employee raised $500M for

## Summary

Alex Wilhelm hosts a double-header on space and purpose-built robotics. First up is Tom Mueller, SpaceX's first employee and now founder/CEO of Impulse Space. He walks through Mira (their in-orbit highly propulsive spacecraft already flying CubeSats and doing rendezvous with Starfish Space) and Helios — "a rocket on top of a rocket" — a third stage that bolts onto Falcon 9 and beats Falcon Heavy on price by about 3x while quadrupling moon payload mass. Tom also discusses why Impulse is staying private despite SpaceX's IPO filing creating a Cambrian explosion of space talent and why the moon — not Mars — matters in the near term (raw materials for megastructures and orbital data centers). He's blunt about why vertical integration matters: the cost-plus space industrial complex builds parts that are too expensive, too late, and "look like Soviet quality."

The second half is Tessa Lau of Dusty Robotics, building wheeled construction robots that print Revit floor plans directly on concrete at 10-17x the speed of a manual layout crew. Dusty is in 150+ deployed robots, working on hospitals and data centers, with a subscription model and a Dusty Academy certification program for operators. Tessa makes a sharp pitch versus the humanoid robotics craze: she says Dusty does more revenue than all humanoids combined, while VCs throw hundreds of millions at companies that haven't shipped. She closes by openly stating her dream of taking Dusty public.
