# The Best Car I've Ever Driven: McLaren W1

## Summary

Marques Brownlee gets to be one of the first people outside McLaren to drive the W1 — the successor to the legendary F1 (still the fastest naturally aspirated car ever at 240 mph) and his personal favorite car of all time, the P1. The pitch: from the powertrain to the aerodynamics to the track performance, the W1 is closer than ever to a literal Formula One car with a license plate. It's a 1275-horsepower hybrid pairing a brand new in-house twin-turbo flat-plane-crank 4-liter V8 (over 900 hp by itself) with a 342-hp electrical system built around a tiny 1.4 kWh battery and a radial flux e-motor straight from Formula E — all in a car whose dry weight is 1399 kg, still under 3200 pounds wet. Against his benchmark Porsche 911 Turbo S, the W1 basically doubles the acceleration numbers: 0 to 186 mph in 12.7 seconds versus about 22.

The design tour is as much aerodynamics lecture as styling review. The active front splitter moves up and down for DRS, air channels through doors that are "basically a bunch of holes," a fake-looking roof scoop exists purely to keep air attached to the top of the car, and the active rear wing extends nearly a foot backward in race mode — becoming an extension of the rear diffuser and, fun fact, no longer street legal because it protrudes past the rear bumper. The air leaves the back of the car twice as fast as it entered the front. Inside, it's McLaren's best interior ever: seats molded into the carbon tub (you adjust the pedals and wheel, not the seat), a CES-concept-style knit material over Bowers & Wilkins speakers, wired Apple CarPlay, two cup holders, and the world's thinnest sun visors.

On tight Italian mountain roads the W1 rides surprisingly well in comfort mode, with the hydraulic steering — in Marques's opinion still the best steering in any supercar — communicating constantly. Then McLaren reserves Mugello Circuit, the F1-grade track that hosted the 2020 Tuscan Grand Prix, for the full 1200-horsepower race-mode experience. The car stayed so locked down that the only looseness was a wiggle braking from 190 mph into turn one — and the G-forces literally pulled the glass inside the camera lens, making footage unusable. At the end of the straight he matched the 190+ mph Lewis Hamilton hit there in an actual Formula One car. With 399 being built at $2 million plus, most of us will only ever see it in videos — but Marques leaves with a new poster car for the bedroom wall.
