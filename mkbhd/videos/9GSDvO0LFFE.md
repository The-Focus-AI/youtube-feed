# The Truth About the "Whoop Killer"

## Summary

Marques Brownlee spends almost two weeks wearing a Whoop 4.0, the new $99 Fitbit Air (formerly the Fitbit Luxe), and an Apple Watch Series 10 at the same time -- through practices, games, workouts, recovery, and sleep -- to answer a question his Ultimate Frisbee teammates keep asking him: is the Fitbit Air a real Whoop killer? The setup matters: he plays at a high level on club and pro Ultimate teams, has a human personal trainer, and treats the experiment as both data collection and personal curiosity.

The verdict has nuance. The Fitbit Air's price model is far friendlier -- $99 to own the device, with no subscription required for basic heart rate, steps, calories, and sleep, vs. Whoop's "the hardware is free but it bricks if you stop paying $200-$350 a year forever." The Fitbit's form factor is slightly smaller and lighter than the Whoop, with easier-to-adjust velcro bands and an AI coach that surprised him by not faltering on tricky questions. But Whoop's feature set is more advanced: a 0-21 strain score, daily stress monitor, journal, advanced labs feature for uploading blood test results, and far more accessories (chest straps, bicep straps, branded clothing, even Whoop underwear). The Apple Watch consistently over-counts calories; the Oura Ring 4 (which he's also wearing) tracks closer to the Whoop on heart rate.

He frames the lineup as a slider: Apple Watch is beginner mode, Fitbit is medium, Whoop is advanced. The Fitbit Air is "a Whoop killer for people who just want more of the basics" -- but with the subscription, it's a Whoop alternative from Google, and Brownlee keeps reminding viewers that this is Google, "a data and ads company" willing to subsidize a tracker to harvest more health data. He hasn't decided which one stays on his wrist, but the teaser is clear: keep watching to see which one wins.
