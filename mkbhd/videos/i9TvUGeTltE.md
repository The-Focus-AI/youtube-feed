# My Take on The New Apple

## Summary

Marques Brownlee unpacks the "Apple turnover" — Tim Cook's announced transition from CEO to Chairman of the Board, with John Ternus, formerly SVP of Hardware Engineering, taking the top job in September. Johny Srouji, the longtime chip-lab leader, moves up to head hardware. Brownlee credits Cook with stepping into "probably the single hardest job in tech" — following Steve Jobs — and using supply-chain and business expertise to take Apple from a trillion-dollar company to a four-trillion-dollar one across 15 years of wildly different economies. But this isn't a one-person shuffle: it's a coordinated wave of long-tenured, 65+ year-old C-suite executives retiring at the same time and handing the reins to a younger generation.

His core thesis is that Apple is moving from a business-CEO era back to a product-CEO era. Steve Jobs was a "product guy" in the nitty-gritty of every device. Tim Cook, by his own showing, wasn't — Brownlee replays the infamous Magic Mouse exchange from his own Cook interview as evidence. Ternus, by contrast, has been visibly paraded around for camera-ready CEO training, hosted the MacBook Neo event in lieu of Cook, and went deep with Brownlee in a separate interview on iPhone materials and Apple's nuanced repairability-vs-durability stance. The recent hardware track record under Ternus's hardware org — Apple Silicon MacBook Pros that are thicker with more battery and ports, the $599 Mac Mini, the $600 MacBook Neo "putting the entire Windows laptop industry on notice" — is offered as the preview of what's coming.

The worry: Apple "almost seems like they avoid the possibility of failure extra hard," which makes them try fewer things. Where Samsung and Google are daily-uploader YouTubers willing to ship into the graveyard, Apple is the every-six-months uploader where every flop — Vision Pro, iPhone 16E, Apple Intelligence, AirPower — echoes for years. Brownlee wants Apple to take more swings: a real Apple camera, a smart-home display speaker, smart glasses, "a printer that doesn't suck." With Ternus reportedly spearheading the upcoming iPhone fold, the hardware future looks promising — but Brownlee closes by flagging that macOS Tahoe, Liquid Glass, Apple Intelligence and Siri all still need to close a serious software gap for the new era to land.
