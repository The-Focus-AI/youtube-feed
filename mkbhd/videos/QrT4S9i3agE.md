# Top 5 Android 17 Features: I Swear It's New!

## Summary

Marques Brownlee runs through his annual top-five features video for the new Android version — with the upfront admission that these releases aren't massive generational overhauls anymore, but collections of small cosmetic tweaks and quality-of-life changes that "back in the day, that would have all been like a dot-one update." The bigger hook is the name: Android 17 is Cinnamon Bun, and Marques is on a one-man campaign to bring the public dessert names back. He walks through the history — Cupcake, Donut, Eclair, Froyo, Gingerbread through Nougat, Oreo, and Pie — notes that Google quietly kept the internal codenames going (Red Velvet Cake, Snow Cone, Tiramisu, Upside Down Cake, Vanilla Ice Cream), and points out the proof that somebody at Google still cares: the system notification glyph in Android 17 is a little cinnamon bun.

The countdown itself: number five is built-in screen reactions — screen recording now has a "show selfie camera" option that keys you in live over the recording. Number four is a game mode for foldables that turns half the screen into the game and the other half into an on-screen gamepad. Number three is the bubble bar, which lets any app launch into a floating window that minimizes into a bubble, with up to four bubbles open at once as a multitasking hub. Number two is a grab bag of aesthetic tweaks: hideable app labels in the stock launcher, tighter spacing in settings, more transparency in the widget picker, per-app dark mode control, and a "You're all caught up" trophy when you clear your notifications. Number one — "Thank God" — is Google finally separating the Wi-Fi and mobile data toggles in quick settings after years of the merged internet toggle.

Along the way there's the new Easter egg (a constellation puzzle that looks complicated but connects in literally any pattern, leading to the returning Asteroids mini-game), a newly customizable permanent search bar on the home screen, and a look ahead at natural-language widget creation, the Rambler speech-to-text upgrade, and the Pause Point digital wellbeing tool. Marques closes by admitting the release makes him want to switch back to the Pixel — before remembering the pain that can turn into — and once again hoping Tensor takes a massive leap forward, though "I've been hurt before, so I'm not going to cross my fingers."
