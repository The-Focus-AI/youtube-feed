# Apple Lost the AI Race

## Summary

Marques Brownlee stages a debate — with studio producer Andrew Manganelli jumping in — over whether Apple lost or won the AI race. The "lost" case is easy to make: when ChatGPT triggered the industry-wide paradigm shift, Google, Meta, and even Bing scrambled to ship, while Apple went silent and "literally refused to say AI in their events for months." Two years of Apple Intelligence promises later, the new Siri had to be rebuilt from the ground up with help from Google. The classic defense — that Apple always sits back, lets early adopters work out the bugs, then steps in late with a polished ecosystem version — gets skewered too: Apple Intelligence so far has been writing tools, AI photo editing, and Genmoji, which the video dismisses as gimmicks nobody buys a phone for.

The counterargument reframes the whole question: the definition of "the AI race" is too narrow. Being OpenAI is one race; making the hardware everyone runs AI on is another. The fastest, most secure generative AI happens on device, and as local models improve, users will go to the cloud less and less — and Apple sells the machines that future runs on. Apple doesn't make search engines either, yet it sells the device you do the Googling on. In that light, Apple Intelligence doesn't have to be great; it just has to be good enough for investors to believe Apple is competitive, because you're still buying an iPhone. Nvidia's push to make ChatRTX a thing shows everyone wants a piece of that hardware pie.

The synthesis: there are two races. In race number one — frontier AI software — Apple "already took the L," and in a world where company A pays company B a billion dollars a year, company B already won. In race number two, the finish line is on the horizon and Apple has a head start because it still makes the iPhone. The new Siri beta shows the ecosystem play (it pulls from iMessage, Calendar, and Photos in a way the ChatGPT app can't), but it doesn't excel at anything ChatGPT or Gemini doesn't already do, can't work in the background, and has far less personal context. The closing question: does an AI company build hardware good enough to pull people off the iPhone, or does Apple make its AI good enough to keep them there?
