# Antigravity A1: A New Take on Drones!

## Summary

Marques Brownlee showcases the Antigravity A1, a sponsored look at a drone that flips the usual formula: instead of a camera gimbal you have to aim while flying, the A1 is essentially a 360 camera that happens to be able to fly. Two lenses — top and bottom, the same sensors and lenses as the Insta360 X3 — capture everything around the drone all the time in 8K/30, and stitching makes the drone body and propellers completely invisible in the final footage. The pitch is that you stop worrying about nailing the shot in the air; you just fly creatively near your subject and do all the framing in post when you get home. Features like Sky Genie (automatic subject orbiting) and Deep Track (car tracking without keyframing) push the tool angle further, and the 249-gram weight keeps it regulatory compliant almost everywhere.

The bigger surprise for Marques is the fun factor. The A1 pairs with FPV goggles that use OmniLink transmission, so you can fly in one direction while freely looking around in any other — "kind of like you're a bird flying through the park." The goggles have a 90-degree field of view (the widest of any flying FPV goggles), 2560x2560 per eye, one-tap defogging, passthrough, and a customizable exterior screen so bystanders can see what you're seeing. Instead of a two-stick controller there's a single joystick: since you're not doing camera moves while flying, you just point the drone and pull the trigger, and it feels closer to flying a plane. Studio producer Andrew went from his first shot in normal flight mode at 10 AM to dynamic sport-mode FPV shots by early afternoon the same day, and Skypath lets a total novice put on the goggles and re-fly a recorded path in real time while looking around in 360.

Even though it's sponsored, Marques asked to include honest feedback: the stitching leaves an ever-so-slight smudge on the horizon line that can sneak into shots, the light-up goggles will get you funny looks in public, and the headset needs a top strap to distribute its weight. Antigravity is also working on goggles-less flight with a ring remote and gesture controls. Battery is 24 minutes standard (39 with the extended version), there's 20 GB of internal storage as a fallback, the lenses are replaceable, and Antigravity Care covers flyaways and water damage. His verdict: this is not a review, but it's one of the most interesting hardware products he's seen in a while.
