# The Windows Laptop Problem

## Summary

Marques Brownlee uses two recent Mac launches -- the $600 MacBook Neo and the new $6,000 16-inch M5 Max MacBook Pro -- as bookends to argue that the Windows laptop industry has a structural problem. The Pro is at the top of every benchmark chart for any Mac ever made, with a GPU more powerful than the M2 Ultra in his $10,000 Mac Pro and SSD speeds approaching 20,000 MB/s. The Neo, meanwhile, is "one of the best deals in tech in a long time" -- a premium-feeling Mac at a price point Windows laptop makers have always owned. He cites a real Asus executive's quote calling the Neo "a shock to the industry" as the springboard.

His thesis: Windows vs. Mac is now structurally similar to Android vs. iPhone. Variety and choice are the upside, but they're also the disadvantage, because every premium Windows laptop relies on Dell (or HP, Asus, Lenovo, Razer) AND Intel AND Microsoft all executing at once. He demos a $2,200 Dell XPS 14 with a Core Ultra 300, 2.8K tandem OLED, and gorgeous metal build -- ruined on first boot by 45 minutes of forced updates and sign-in flows, plus a McAfee pop-up ad. The Copilot key, Recall, and "AI in Microsoft Paint, for crying out loud" round out the indictment of Microsoft as the weak link.

At the low end, where laptops like the Acer Aspire 16 ($550) used to thrive on trade-offs, the Neo brings vertical integration to the price point too. And, crucially, Apple isn't trying to make hardware margin on the Neo -- it's a Trojan horse to acquire first-time Mac buyers who become subscribers to TV+, AppleCare, iCloud and other services. Tim Cook tweeted that Mac just had its best launch ever for first-time customers. If Windows OEMs can't respond, the Neo is going to "keep eating their lunch."
