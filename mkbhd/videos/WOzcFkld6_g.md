# The Most Interesting Displays In The World!

## Summary

Marques Brownlee rounds up the most interesting display tech to come through the studio, starting with the Magic Screen by Intricate — a touch-sensitive flexible glass panel that snaps onto a MacBook with magnets, plugs in over USB-C, and gives the Mac a full corner-to-corner touchscreen by literally pretending to be a trackpad. The accessory is surprisingly well thought out (folio stand, travel tuck, a corner USB-C port that doubles as a stopper, and a pressure-sensitive stylus with a 100-hour battery), but it lives under the shadow of the rumored OLED touchscreen MacBook Pro. Marques argues that rumor actually helps the product: when Apple ships a touchscreen laptop, macOS will finally get touch-friendly UI, and every one of these $150-ish Kickstarter add-ons will get easier to use along with it.

Next is a volumetric display — the "hologram" — built from two display panels spinning back to back at 900 RPM. Because a full rotation is divided into 480 unique slices, showing a 3D object at just 30 FPS requires updating the displays 7,200 times per second, plus extra processing headroom for error correction since the motors don't spin at exactly 900 RPM. Marques calls it one of the most interesting pieces of tech that's ever come through the studio. For desks rather than showpieces, he covers Dell's 52-inch ultra-wide 6K slightly curved 120Hz Thunderbolt monitor — the first single monitor he thinks could genuinely replace his dual-display setup, thanks to 129 PPI pixel density, a built-in KVM switch for up to four computers, picture-by-picture screen partitioning, and a 140W Thunderbolt 4 connection with 2.5-gig Ethernet. At $3,000, "it might cost two displays, but it kind of is two displays."

The finale is an exclusive first look at Project Aura, a collaboration between Xreal, Google, and Qualcomm that sits squarely between smart glasses and a VR headset. The glasses run Android XR as their own computer, wired to a smartphone-sized puck running the Snapdragon AR2 Gen 1 chip — a "split computing" design that makes the frames just 91 grams and lets you upgrade the puck or the frames independently. The micro-OLED displays are 1920x1200 per eye at 72 FPS with a 70-degree field of view, the glass has electrochromic tint that blacks out at the click of a button, and tracking latency is literally three milliseconds versus around 12 on the Vision Pro. Less than a year after "Wait, Smart Glasses are Actually Good Now?", Marques marvels at how much choice the face-computer space suddenly has.
