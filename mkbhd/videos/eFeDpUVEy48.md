# “The Biggest Android Update Ever”

## Summary

Marques Brownlee unpacks Google's pre-I/O Android show, where Google billed the announcements as "the biggest Android update ever." He walks through Android 17, a new Android Auto, and the rebranded "Google Books" Chromebooks, separating the genuinely useful from the overhyped. While Android 17 isn't a visual redesign, it adds practical features like an expanded Autofill that can pull data (including passport numbers) from Google Photos, a digital wellbeing tool called Pause Point, better speech-to-text via Rambler, and Gemini-powered creator tools built into the OS.

Brownlee is openly skeptical of the agentic Gemini Intelligence pitch — particularly a promo showing a "book two floor seats to this concert" one-click button. He argues that nobody is going to trust an AI to handle ticket purchases without verification steps, and that the demo as shown is essentially advertising a one-click flow that doesn't actually exist. He's also critical of Google's new Smart Enhance photo processing, calling the before/after a flatter, less contrasty result that he says actually looks worse than the original.

The more useful wins, in his view, are the smaller features: custom temporary widgets generated by natural language, Android Auto's visual overhaul with 3D building silhouettes and lane guidance, full-screen HD YouTube playback while parked, and the new AI-enabled cursor on Google Books that turns the pointer into a multi-modal Gemini portal. He closes with cautious optimism — the utility-focused updates feel real, while the agentic promises will need to be tested.
