# So This is Peak Smartphone

## Summary

Using the Oppo Find X9 Ultra as a launching point, Marques Brownlee argues we have arrived at "peak slab phone" -- the moving goalpost has effectively been reached for the traditional non-folding flagship. He frames the case through his five pillars (display, battery, build, performance, camera) and walks through how each of the first four watermarks has been substantively achieved: large flat bright OLEDs with one-nit lows and high-frequency PWM dimming; silicon-carbon batteries pushing 6,000-7,000+ mAh that turn flagships into two-day phones; Snapdragon 8 Elite Gen 5-class chips with smooth software; and IP69 metal-rail builds with slightly improved-but-still-glass screens.

The unsolved pillar is camera, and Brownlee argues it can never be fully solved -- it's a battle against physics. The X9 Ultra makes the case anyway: a 200MP main, a 200MP 3x telephoto (bigger than most phones' main sensor), a 50MP 10x periscope, a 50MP ultrawide, a dedicated True Color sensor, and a Hasselblad partnership. He owns the actual Hasselblad X2D Mark II that this phone is supposedly modeled after and reminds viewers they're "opposite cameras" -- the X2D has no computational photography and not even a video mode, while the X9 Ultra exists to "minimize the misses and maximize the makes" no matter the user's skill.

The conclusion: the camera watermark has shifted from "as good as a real camera" to "takes a really good photo or video any time," and we're effectively there. Plus Hasselblad/Zeiss-style lens attachments and battery grips push it further. He compares it to the iPad-as-laptop debate -- versatile, "good enough" for nearly everyone, but never a true replacement for the dedicated tool.
