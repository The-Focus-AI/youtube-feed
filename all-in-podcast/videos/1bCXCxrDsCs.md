# SpaceX-Cursor Deal, SaaS Debt Bomb, New Apple CEO, SPLC Indictment, Colon Cancer Spike

## Summary

The pod opens on Trump's All-In appearance, Sacks's PCAST role, and what Sacks calls one of the luckier facts of the AI moment — that Trump is the president "who doesn't have these doomer neuroses" about AI. Chamath plays out the alt-history: under a Kamala administration, "we'd have no data centers and they'd be using AI to censor us." The crew then unpacks SpaceX's reported $60B Cursor acquisition, a deal so deliberately structured around an existing S-1 that the announcement was tied to S-1 staleness windows. Sacks lays out cyber as the next AI battlefield — Anthropic's Mythos is "10 trillion parameters" and possibly too compute-hungry to serve, opening a window for cheaper, Mythos-comparable models from competitors.

The SaaS apocalypse continues with a deep dive on debt as the structural killer of the next wave of public software companies. Chamath drops the line of the year — "Debt equals prison bitch, keep it in your mind" — and tells his own story of a $420M credit line collapsing inward. They run through Medallia at 18% of sales target, Salesforce trading at sub-10x free cash flow, and Benioff's "headless product" pivot, before turning to Apple's CEO succession. Tim Cook's shareholder-return record gets full credit; John Ternus is set up as the future. The Disney parallel — Eisner's "vault strategy" giving way to Iger's Pixar/Marvel/Lucasfilm acquisitions — frames the question of whether the next Apple CEO is an operator or a balance-sheet buyer.

The back half is dominated by the SPLC grand-jury indictment and the question of how the org accumulated "$822 million in offshore bank accounts." Sacks methodically walks through the cover story (paying informants) versus the indictment's claim that the SPLC was paying leaders of designated groups. Friedberg's surprise science corner closes the show with a Barcelona epigenetics study linking the herbicide picloram to the 80% rise in colorectal cancer in under-50s — a federal-funding-of-fundamental-science success story they argue should not be a one-off.
