# The State of Modern War: Palantir & Anduril Execs on Drones, AI, and the End of Traditional Warfare

## Summary

Recorded at the Hill and Valley Forum, Jason Calacanis and Chamath Palihapitiya sit down with Anduril co-founder Trae Stephens and Palantir CTO Shyam Sankar to dissect the resurgence of defense technology in Silicon Valley. The pair walk through the post-9/11 origin story of Palantir — five co-founders, including Peter Thiel, fighting to break the "monopsony" of the Department of Defense — and how Anduril extended that playbook from software into hardware. The throughline is a rejection of the legacy "cost-plus" model: rather than building to government specs and billing the taxpayer, both companies bet private R&D capital on products and force the government to compete on merits.

Much of the conversation focuses on the alarming erosion of the American industrial base. The hosts surface a 10,000-to-1 drone production gap with China, a 232x shipbuilding capacity disadvantage, and Chamath's claim that the U.S. has only eight days of munitions on hand for a major conflict versus the 800 days needed. Trae and Shyam argue that "deterrence is the factory, not the stockpile" — that what matters in a long war is software-defined, modular manufacturing capacity that can pivot quickly between weapons systems, not a small fleet of "exquisite" multi-million-dollar interceptors. They detail Anduril's Arsenal-1 campus in Columbus and contrast SpaceX's Starship ($20/kg to orbit) with the Space Shuttle ($50,000/kg) as proof that vertically integrated, product-led innovation outperforms politically distributed defense projects (the F-35 has components made in 400 congressional districts).

The episode closes with hard ethical and cultural questions: Anthropic's recent refusal to allow Claude to be used in Project Maven without human oversight, accusations of Palantir powering a "surveillance state," and the lingering cultural rift between Silicon Valley and the Pentagon that started in Vietnam. Trae and Shyam defend autonomous decision-making as the "third offset" that actually improves the ethics of war by reducing civilian casualties, and the besties discuss declassified evidence of Soviet (and now adversarial) funding of U.S. anti-war and domestic protest movements as part of the broader contest for American sovereignty.

- **The monopsony problem** ([3:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjNP5IOzCbA&t=180s)) — Defense's single-customer government structure killed product innovation; Anduril and Palantir model breaks it via private R&D
- **From 51 to 5 primes** ([17:26](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjNP5IOzCbA&t=1046s)) — The defense industry consolidated from 51 major contractors down to 5–6 primes who make subs of subs of subs
- **Power-law of defense innovation** ([18:24](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=mjNP5IOzCbA&t=1104s)) — Like venture, defense innovation will be a power law: Anduril, SpaceX, OpenAI, and one or two others

### Concepts
- **Cost-plus contracting** — Legacy DoD model; bills government for cost plus margin, kills product innovation
- **The primes** — 5–6 dominant defense contractors that emerged from 51-firm consolidation
- **Attritable mass** — Low-cost, high-volume autonomous systems (drones, munitions) you can lose without strategic harm
- **Third offset** — Decision advantage via AI/autonomy after nuclear (1st) and stealth/precision (2nd) offsets
- **Inspector General** — Statutory independent investigators in every federal agency
- **Heretics and heroes** — Chamath's framing for individuals who break bureaucratic sclerosis
