# Senator John Fetterman: Country Over Party

## Summary

Senator John Fetterman joins Jason Calacanis, David Friedberg, and David Sacks for a wide-ranging interview on his political evolution from progressive to self-described "plain Democrat" who prioritizes moral clarity and country over party. Fetterman addresses his unusually high approval ratings among Pennsylvania Republicans (around 60%) and his deteriorating standing with the progressive wing, attributing the shift to his unapologetic support for Israel, his refusal to shut down the Department of Homeland Security, and his willingness to agree with the other side when he believes they are right. He offers a blunt critique of the Democratic Party, arguing it is currently governed by "Trump Derangement Syndrome" (TDS) that prevents lawmakers from acknowledging good outcomes simply because they come from a Republican president.

On foreign policy, Fetterman is the sole Democrat in Congress publicly celebrating the U.S.-Israel military campaign against Iran, calling it a justified dismantlement of a dangerous regime and criticizing NATO allies for refusing to help reopen the Strait of Hormuz despite consuming oil. Domestically, he supports the concept of voter ID -- noting 83% of Americans favor it -- but opposes the SAVE Act in its current form because Republicans loaded it with partisan add-ons instead of keeping it simple. On immigration, he describes himself as pro-immigration but insists the border must be secured and criminals deported, calling the Biden administration's handling of the border a serious failure that cost Democrats the 2024 election.

The conversation also touches on the national debt death spiral, government fraud, the wealth tax proposals from Bernie Sanders and Ro Khanna, and the state of American agriculture. Fetterman dismisses radical progressive proposals like AI data center moratoriums as gifts to China and refuses to demonize billionaires. Throughout the interview, he positions himself as an independent voice willing to "call balls and strikes" rather than engage in what he calls the "attention economy" of modern partisan politics.
