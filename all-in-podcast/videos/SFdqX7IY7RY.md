# OpenAI's Identity Crisis, Datacenter Wars, Market Up on Iran News, Mamdani's First Tax, Swalwell Out

## Summary

The full core four are back, with Friedberg sitting "next door to David Sacks" — not doxxed, the besties insist, because Ken Griffin's address has been public since the marketing materials. The opening is a meditation on Mamdani-style mansion taxes, London's non-dom collapse, San Francisco and LA's transfer-tax dry spells, and pied-à-terre culture. Sacks frames the macro point — when a city loses the rule of law and adopts arbitrary taxes, capital simply flees to the next jurisdiction. Chamath disagrees on aesthetics ("I'm not a big fan of pied-à-terre culture"), but agrees on the structural risk to New York.

The center of gravity is the OpenAI-vs-Anthropic flip in secondary markets, Anthropic now priced higher despite OpenAI's rumored $850B valuation. Codex is "really good" but the besties argue OpenAI must isolate consumer and enterprise to avoid context switching. Compute is the choke point: Elon's Colossus is going to 555,000 GPUs, Meta's Prometheus to 150,000, and Elon just struck a hyperscaler-style deal with Crusoe to rent capacity. Maine just banned data-center construction outright, 40% of contested data centers are being canceled, and Chamath calls Allbirds' insane 450% rally a canary signal — "we are absolutely compute constrained." For Anthropic and OpenAI, "it's a five-alarm fire" to lock down land, power, shell, and chips.

The back half: Eric Swalwell drops out of California's gubernatorial race in a Biden-2024-style coordinated retreat; Sacks reads the text-message coordination across the Democratic establishment. Chamath delivers the lasting line — "Nancy Pelosi, Stan Druckenmiller, and Warren Buffett are three of the most accomplished investors of all time" — and refuses to back off on his Buffett-pre-vs-post-Reg-FD analysis. The episode closes with The Price David Sacks Is Wrong gameshow (Bored Apes, Allbirds, Theranos) and Friedberg's deflating verdict on agentic investing: agents on Kalshi and Polymarket "are just not that smart yet" — they have no taste, can't do anything novel, and require human-in-the-loop.
