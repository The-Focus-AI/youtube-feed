# "This is Bibi's War" - Harvard's Graham Allison on the Influences and Endgame of the Iran War

## Summary

Harvard Professor Graham Allison returns to the All-In Podcast for a sweeping strategic analysis of the most pressing geopolitical crises facing the world. Allison, the founding dean of the Harvard Kennedy School who has advised every Secretary of Defense since Kissinger, delivers a blunt assessment of the ongoing U.S.-Israeli military campaign against Iran: he calls it "Bibi's war," arguing that Israeli Prime Minister Netanyahu has been obsessively pursuing the destruction of the Iranian regime for two decades and finally succeeded in drawing the Trump administration into the conflict. While praising the extraordinary military and intelligence capabilities on display, Allison warns that destroying a regime is far easier than building a stable replacement, citing the costly failures of Iraq and Afghanistan as cautionary precedents. He sees Trump as likely to declare victory soon -- possibly before his scheduled China trip on March 29th -- but notes that Netanyahu wants to fight on until the regime is fully destroyed.

The conversation then pivots to China, Taiwan, Greenland, and nuclear proliferation. On Taiwan, Allison puts the probability of a Chinese invasion at roughly 5% for the near term, citing China's ongoing military leadership purge, its theory of "peaceful reunification," and the fact that Trump is the most accommodating U.S. president China is likely to see on the Taiwan question. He introduces his powerful "80-80-9" framework: 80 years without a great power war, 80 years without a nuclear weapon used in combat, and only 9 nuclear-armed states -- a record he describes as historically unprecedented and dangerously fragile. On Greenland, he argues the U.S. can get everything it needs through long-term leasing rather than acquisition, and characterizes Trump's approach as part reality TV show, part strategic maneuvering.

The episode closes with a sobering discussion of domestic risk: rising wealth inequality in the United States. Allison warns that when the top 10-20% capture 70-80% of economic gains, the resulting resentment is "not stable and sustainable in a democracy," creating fertile ground for radical populism and socialism. He urges those at the top to recognize they have the most to lose and to think more seriously about redistribution before the political system forces more extreme solutions.
