# Anthropic's Fable Backlash, Nationalizing AI, Inflation Heats Up & California's Broken Elections

## Summary

The original quartet is back for a debate-heavy episode that opens on Anthropic's new Mythos-class model, Fable 5. It tops nearly every benchmark, but the developer world revolted over how it ships: mandatory 30-day retention of every prompt, output, and context window — with no exceptions, even for enterprise customers holding zero-data-retention agreements — plus silent "nerfing" that kicks users down to a lesser model when Anthropic decides they aren't worthy of frontier capability. Sacks frames it as the culmination of a "very sophisticated regulatory capture campaign," Friedberg walks through how the biotech restrictions are already pushing his own company (Ohalo) toward Chinese open-source models, and Chamath live-demos getting himself downgraded twice for asking innocuous questions about fertilizer regulation and mitochondria. The group lands on a shared thesis: the intervention should happen at the level of weaponized *output* (they praise the mandatory nucleic-acid synthesis screening letter) rather than gatekeeping who gets to use the model at all.

From there the conversation turns to Bernie Sanders' New York Times op-ed proposing an American AI Sovereign Wealth Fund — a one-time 50% equity tax on OpenAI, Anthropic, and xAI. Sacks won't endorse the confiscation but admits he has sympathy for it, arguing the AI CEOs "asked for it" by fear-mongering about 50% job loss while training for free on humanity's collective knowledge. Friedberg counters that there is no job-loss apocalypse — AI's real leverage is on the revenue side, and he'd rather see Social Security restructured into an account-based, equity-owning sovereign wealth fund. Chamath adds the key economic observation that AI, unlike the internet, has a large marginal cost per user, which is the strongest argument for the government owning a slice of the labs. The besties riff on "trillycorns," "capitalist cucks," and paying down the national debt with seized equity.

The back third covers a hot inflation print — CPI at 4.2% and PPI at 6.5%, multi-year highs that Friedberg and Chamath tie to the Iran/Ukraine war energy shock and out-of-control government spending — before a long, heated segment on the Los Angeles mayoral primary. Friedberg lays out the statistical anomaly in the late mail-in ballots and argues California's stack of laws (unlimited ballot harvesting, universal mail ballots, no voter ID) has turned elections into "appointments." Sacks calls it "legalized fraud"; Chamath calls the odds "one in a trillion" but presses everyone to steelman it, eventually surfacing the box-out theory that late voters coordinated to keep Spencer Pratt off the general ballot. The recurring theme across all four topics: eroding trust in centralized institutions and the case for open, transparent, ID-verified alternatives.

- **The KYC "tell"** ([28:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gH4FTjDm9FQ&t=1699s)) - Friedberg argues Anthropic could implement know-your-customer verification tomorrow; the fact that it hasn't reveals the regulatory-capture agenda.
- **Dario's call for an FDA/FAA for models** ([16:03](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=gH4FTjDm9FQ&t=963s)) - Sacks: even as Anthropic surveils and nerfs, Dario blogged that transparency isn't enough and a new agency should approve all models.
