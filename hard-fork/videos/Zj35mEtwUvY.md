# OpenAI Vs. Anthropic: How the Pentagon Picked Its Partner

## Summary

This emergency episode of Hard Fork covers the chaotic 48-hour period in which the Pentagon's standoff with Anthropic reached a dramatic conclusion. After Anthropic CEO Dario Amodei refused to budge on two red lines -- no domestic mass surveillance and no fully autonomous weapons -- President Trump ordered all federal agencies to stop using Anthropic's technology. Then, in a severe escalation, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth posted on X that Anthropic would be designated a "supply chain risk," a punitive measure typically reserved for foreign adversaries like Chinese chip suppliers or Kaspersky Lab, effectively barring any military contractor from doing business with Anthropic.

Within 24 hours of Anthropic's deal collapsing, Sam Altman announced that OpenAI had reached its own agreement with the Pentagon to deploy models in classified networks, claiming the deal included the same two red lines Anthropic had insisted on. Kevin and Casey dig into the critical difference: while OpenAI says the Pentagon "agrees with these principles" and "reflects them in law and policy," Anthropic argues that the legal language accompanying similar concessions would have rendered them meaningless -- because the US lacks a national privacy law and data brokers already make de facto mass surveillance legal. The hosts frame this as potentially the most punitive action the US government has taken against a major American company this century, drawing comparisons to how the Chinese government regulates its tech sector.

The episode also examines the broader implications for Silicon Valley, employee activism at AI labs, the question of regulatory capture, and the chilling effect on companies that might resist government demands. Casey notes that Dario Amodei has been preparing for exactly this kind of conflict, having long asked Anthropic employees to read "The Making of the Atomic Bomb" to understand the moral stakes of building powerful AI.

## Key Points

- **Anthropic's two red lines** ([1:09](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Zj35mEtwUvY&t=69s)) - Anthropic refused to allow its models to be used for domestic mass surveillance or fully autonomous weapons, leading to a crisis with the Pentagon
- **Dario Amodei's public statement** ([3:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Zj35mEtwUvY&t=180s)) - On February 26th, Amodei posted on Anthropic's website that they would not compromise, invoking "conscience" -- a word no tech leader had used since Trump's reelection
- **Trump's Truth Social order** ([4:06](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Zj35mEtwUvY&t=246s)) - President Trump directed all federal agencies to cease using Anthropic's technology with a six-month phase-out period, but notably did not invoke the supply chain risk designation or the Defense Production Act
- **Hegseth's X escalation** ([6:23](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Zj35mEtwUvY&t=383s)) - An hour after Trump's post, Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth posted on X designating Anthropic a supply chain risk, barring all military contractors from commercial activity with the company
- **Sam Altman's solidarity then pivot** ([7:31](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Zj35mEtwUvY&t=451s)) - On Thursday, Altman told OpenAI employees they stood with Anthropic; by Friday night, OpenAI announced its own Pentagon deal
- **The "all lawful use" standard** ([9:52](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Zj35mEtwUvY&t=592s)) - The core conflict: the Pentagon wanted companies to agree their AI could be used for anything legal, but the US lacks meaningful AI regulation and a national privacy law
- **Social media immigration dragnet** ([9:52](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Zj35mEtwUvY&t=592s)) - Casey notes that federal agencies are already using social media surveillance to screen immigrants, technically legal but functionally equivalent to mass surveillance
- **Anthropic offered similar deal with "legalese"** ([13:53](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Zj35mEtwUvY&t=833s)) - Amodei said Anthropic was offered similar concessions but accompanied by legalese that would have made them ineffective
- **Two possible explanations** ([14:27](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Zj35mEtwUvY&t=867s)) - Either this is purely a political vendetta against Anthropic, or OpenAI agreed to things Anthropic would not
- **"Attempted corporate murder"** ([15:52](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Zj35mEtwUvY&t=952s)) - Former Trump administration member Dean Ball characterized the government's actions against Anthropic as "attempted corporate murder" based on ideology
- **Employee activism across AI labs** ([18:08](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Zj35mEtwUvY&t=1088s)) - An open letter signed by employees of OpenAI, Google DeepMind, and other labs expressed solidarity with Anthropic's stance against surveillance and autonomous weapons
- **OpenAI's "safety stack" claim** ([19:51](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Zj35mEtwUvY&t=1191s)) - Sam Altman said OpenAI would build protections into the model itself to prevent misuse, but critics compare this to OpenAI's unfulfilled promises about Sora safeguards
- **The data broker loophole** ([21:17](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Zj35mEtwUvY&t=1277s)) - It is legal for federal agencies to buy Americans' data from data brokers, making de facto mass surveillance possible without violating any law
