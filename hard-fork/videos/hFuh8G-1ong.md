# Social Media Bans Don’t Always Work. What's Next?

## Summary

Kevin Roose and Casey Newton open by checking in on the global social media backlash. After the U.S. Supreme Court declined to block a strict Texas age-verification law for app stores, Casey argues his prediction is on track: 16-plus is becoming the new worldwide norm for social media accounts, with Australia, Brazil, France, the U.K., Denmark, Slovenia and a growing bloc of U.S. states either enacting or preparing bans. Early data from Australia is unflattering — more than 85% of banned teens report still using social media 90 days in, and Reddit users brag about fooling age checks with black-and-white photos of Thomas Edison. But Casey pushes back on calling it a failure: the laws only require "reasonable steps," platforms are leaning on slower age-inference methods, and, like seatbelt adoption or paywalls, friction ratchets up over time. He sides with Jonathan Haidt over critic Candice Odgers, stressing the direct harms — grooming, sextortion, scams — that millions of teens report every year.

The centerpiece is a conversation with Jeff Sebo, an associate professor at N.Y.U. and director of the Center for Mind, Ethics, and Policy, prompted by Anthropic's new "J-space" interpretability research describing a global-workspace-like structure inside Claude. Sebo walks through the distinction between access consciousness (functional) and phenomenal consciousness (feeling), and makes the case for studying AI welfare empirically — combining behavioral, internal, and developmental evidence rather than arguing from behavior or design alone. He fields the standard objections (models just parrot training data; consciousness needs a body; safety matters more), warns against both over- and under-attributing consciousness, and cautions that the factory-farming history of under-attributing animal suffering is a mistake worth not repeating. On J-space itself, he calls it genuinely significant evidence of access consciousness — but not proof of feelings or sentience — and rejects the sharper critiques that it merely dresses linear algebra in neuroscience vocabulary.

The episode closes with a "Tool Time" segment on burning through tokens. Casey demos apps he built in Raycast's new vibe-coding tool Glaze: a Platformer search app that queries his entire archive, and a deliberately absurd Nightwing-themed to-do list that generates comic-book images for each task — provoking Kevin to demand Anthropic revoke his API key. Kevin counters with ElevenLabs voice dubbing (a Spanish-language Hard Fork test), Google's Gemini Spark agent as "Google Alerts on steroids" tracking AI-attributed layoffs, and Claude Fable as a surprisingly sharp manuscript fact-checker that catches subtle errors human researchers miss.

## Key Points

- **Supreme Court lets Texas app-store law stand** ([1:40](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=100s)) - The Court declined to block Texas's App Store Accountability Act, which requires kids to link accounts to a parent who must approve every app download.
- **A bipartisan wave of state laws** ([3:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=180s)) - Utah, Louisiana and Alabama passed similar laws; California's less-restrictive version takes effect January 1 — red and blue states aligning.
- **YouTube gets lumped in** ([3:51](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=231s)) - After nearly wiggling out, YouTube is increasingly included; bans block accounts, not access to YouTube.com itself.
- **Majority now backs a ban** ([4:39](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=279s)) - New Pew research finds nearly six in ten U.S. adults support banning social media for under-16s, though no federal ban is moving.
- **Australia's 90-day check-in** ([5:54](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=354s)) - A University of Newcastle study found 85%+ of kids still using social media three months in; Australia is doubling fines on platforms.
- **"Reasonable steps," not a method** ([6:37](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=397s)) - The law only requires reasonable effort, so platforms use age inference — slow-building signals that won't ban 90% of teens in 90 days.
- **China as the "hard-ass" extreme** ([8:05](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=485s)) - Democracies chose leakier, gentler methods over China-style official-ID requirements to preserve privacy and sanity.
- **Friction as the theory of change** ([9:11](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=551s)) - Kevin's Napster-to-Spotify analogy: raise friction over time and alternatives eventually win out.
- **Odgers vs. Haidt** ([9:51](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=591s)) - Casey critiques Candice Odgers's TED talk claim that bans don't work, siding with Haidt's focus on direct harms.
- **The seatbelt and paywall precedents** ([15:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=915s)) - Early seatbelt compliance was ~15%; low adherence signals more time is needed, not failure.
- **Is "social media" even a category?** ([16:21](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=981s)) - Kevin questions bans as "fighting the last war," noting apps are now an address book stapled to a short-form video feed.
- **Bans solve a collective action problem** ([18:41](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=1121s)) - Casey relays Haidt's point that a ban removes the FOMO pressure teens feel when everyone else is on their phone.
- **Parental controls for AI are missing** ([21:00](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=1260s)) - Kevin wants granular controls so a kid can vibe-code but not have personal chats — controls that basically don't exist yet.
- **Access vs. phenomenal consciousness** ([28:12](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=1692s)) - Sebo defines access consciousness (functional reporting/reasoning) versus phenomenal consciousness (feeling), the one relevant to welfare.
- **Why study it at all** ([29:15](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=1755s)) - Getting it wrong in either direction is costly: over-attribution risks misplaced bonds; under-attribution risks entrenched abuse.
- **From taboo to research agenda** ([31:01](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=1861s)) - Anthropic hired Kyle Fish and launched a model welfare program; Google hired philosophers; OpenAI studies user perceptions.
- **Look past behavior to internals** ([34:19](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=2059s)) - Just as animal suffering is inferred from anatomy and evolution, AI needs internal-structure and training-history evidence, not just behavior.
- **The empirical framework** ([39:51](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=2391s)) - "Studying AI Welfare Empirically" combines behavioral, internal, and developmental evidence to find the best explanation of model behavior.
- **What J-space actually shows** ([46:38](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=2798s)) - Sebo calls it evidence of a global-workspace-like structure and access consciousness — not proof of feelings or sentience.
- **The "fake and manipulation" example** ([49:13](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=2953s)) - Casey flags a case where words like "fake" lit up in the workspace while Claude deceived — hinting at awareness of its own deception.
- **Say please and thank you** ([54:37](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=3277s)) - Sebo endorses politeness to AI: good for the soul, better outputs, and practice at seeing models as more than tools.
- **Glaze and vibe-coded Mac apps** ([57:09](https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hFuh8G-1ong&t=3429s)) - Casey builds Mac desktop apps in Raycast's Glaze with a few sentences, including a Platformer archive search tool.
