# Why the A.I. Backlash Turned Violent in America

## Summary

This episode of Hard Fork tackles the rising and increasingly violent backlash against AI in America. Kevin Roose and Casey Newton open with the news that an attacker targeted Sam Altman's home in San Francisco and shots were fired at the Indianapolis councilman Ron Gibson's house after he voted in favor of a data center. The hosts use these incidents as a launching pad to examine why AI and data centers have become so unpopular: the state of Maine recently passed a temporary moratorium on data centers above 20 megawatts, towns from Wisconsin to North Carolina are voting against them, and Bernie Sanders and AOC have proposed a national moratorium. Kevin walks through Stanford's 2024 AI index showing only 31% of Americans trust their government to regulate AI responsibly -- about half the global average.

The hosts then examine three explanations for the backlash. The first, advanced by Sam Altman in a personal blog post, is that journalism critical of AI is making leaders less safe. Casey pushes back that the bigger issue is OpenAI lobbying against transparency bills, sending subpoenas to nonprofit AI critics, and backing an Illinois bill that would shield it from liability. The second explanation is economic: people are afraid AI will take their jobs or make their existing jobs miserable, and the AI companies have no convincing answer beyond "the government will figure it out." The third is anti-elitism: AI is a top-down project being deployed at unprecedented speed by a handful of unelected people who actively fight regulation. Kevin argues data center NIMBYism is misdirected -- the real levers are policies like flexible safety nets and worker retraining -- but Casey notes saying no in your neighborhood is the only lever average Americans actually have.

In the second segment, Kara Swisher returns to discuss her new CNN docuseries "Kara Swisher Wants to Live Forever," in which she explores Silicon Valley's obsession with longevity. Kara dismisses much of what she tried -- hyperbaric chambers, ketamine, sound therapy -- as "the rich people" playing at perfectibility while ignoring the simplest interventions like universal healthcare. She does endorse VO2 max training, CRISPR for sickle cell, and mRNA technology for cancer. She delivers a passionate critique of the inequity of biohacking culture in the backdrop of cut research funding, and reveals that on ketamine she "thought only about Casey... naked." The third segment, on Mark Zuckerberg's attempt to build an AI version of himself to interact with Meta staff, closes the episode.
