# Fable 5 is Back ... But Why Was It Gone at All?

## Summary

Hard Fork returns from break to unpack what Kevin Roose and Casey Newton call an "unprecedented" shift in how the U.S. government treats frontier AI. After the Commerce Department abruptly used export controls to pull Anthropic's Fable 5 and Mythos 5 off the market on June 12 — reportedly after Amazon flagged a jailbreak to the administration — the restrictions were quietly lifted weeks later, with Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick declaring the risks addressed. Meanwhile OpenAI was pressured to hold GPT-5.6 back to a government-approved list of partners. The hosts diagnose a "de facto licensing regime" with no published rules, no transparency, and no due process — AI, in Kevin's phrase, "regulated by vibes" — and note the irony that the tech-right figures who once decried a Biden-era "licensing scheme" have now built exactly that. They also debate the China angle: whether yanking powerful American models off the market cedes ground to Chinese open-source models like Zhipu AI's GLM 5.2, or whether it starves the very distillation pipeline those models depend on.

The middle segment is an interview with Dr. Dana Suskind — pediatric cochlear-implant surgeon, University of Chicago professor, founder of the TMW Center, and mother of eight — about her forthcoming book "Human Raised." Suskind argues AI should enhance parenting, never replace the human connection that wires young brains, and offers two frameworks: HOPE (a values compass) and DETECT (a six-question checklist for vetting AI products). She likens AI to processed food — a spectrum from nourishing to ultra-processed "slop" — draws a hard line against AI companions and toys for young kids on precautionary grounds, praises Norway's move to keep gen-AI out of early schooling, and warns that a "human-raised" childhood could become a luxury good while artificial alternatives become the "cheap calories of brain nutrition."

The episode closes with a new recurring segment, "Against All Odds," a prediction-markets roundup. The hosts skewer a Rick Rubin-scored Polymarket ad, recount the absurd "Donking of a Truth Machine" saga (a market resolved by whoever amassed the most UMA tokens over whether a caster said "donk"), cite a Wall Street Journal finding that paid Polymarket creator videos showed fake winning bets that would really have lost $166,000, and dissect NYT reporting that Mark Zuckerberg has dispatched a team to build a Polymarket-style app internally called "Arena."
