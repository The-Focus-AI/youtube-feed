# Sundar Pichai on A.I. Backlash, the Future of Work and Google's Next Era

## Summary

Recorded the day after Google I/O 2026, Kevin Roose and Casey Newton sit down with Google CEO Sundar Pichai for a wide-ranging interview about Google's position in the AI race. Pichai concedes that while Google is "the only large company actually at that frontier," it's behind in specific areas — particularly agentic coding, tool use, and long-horizon developer tasks where Anthropic and OpenAI have pulled ahead. He emphasizes the rapid iteration enabled by Gemini 3.5 Flash and the surprising internal usage numbers, noting that Google's own token consumption is "doubling every week." On the bumpy launch-day reception of 3.5 Flash, with complaints about pricing and quality, he acknowledges regressions and says rate limits were tightened to avoid outages but will loosen soon.

The conversation turns to the broader AI backlash — including a recent NYT/Siena poll finding 35% of Americans think AI is "mostly bad" — and the trend of commencement speakers being booed for mentioning AI. Pichai is sympathetic to public anxiety, framing it as a natural response to "the most profound technology humanity will ever work on" arriving faster than humans evolved to handle change. He pushes back on overly deterministic dire scenarios about jobs, comparing AI to the spreadsheet revolution and arguing that radiologists, doctors, and graduates will be more productive and freer for human work rather than displaced. He's heading to Stanford to give a commencement speech and brushes off the boo risk with optimism about the next generation.

On the policy and safety side, Pichai signals support for the rumored Trump executive order requiring government pre-release review of AI models, calling cross-industry coordination on cybersecurity "prudent" while warning against slowing the U.S. down at the frontier. On recursive self-improvement (RSI), he says Google sees agents building things like simple operating systems in 12 hours (work that would take humans thousands), but the field is "not quite there yet" on true RSI — and if a lab approaches that moment, "we all have to avoid race conditions." He defends Google's decision to sell TPUs to rivals like Anthropic as the economics of running a platform business — Anthropic researchers using TPUs help Google improve its next-generation chips. Asked if he's "AGI-pilled," Pichai says the rate of progress over the last 1–2 years has made him feel AGI is "on the closer side" of the 3–10 year range, and he endorses Demis Hassabis's "foothills of the singularity" framing as essentially "the advent of AGI" by around 2030.
