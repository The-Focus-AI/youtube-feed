# OpenAI Trial: The 5 Most Surprising Moments

## Summary

In this short bonus segment, Kevin Roose and Casey Newton break down their top five most surprising moments from the ongoing Elon Musk vs. OpenAI trial. The countdown starts at #5 with Sam Altman's testimony about a "hair-raising chat" in which Musk, asked what would happen to OpenAI if he died, said he hadn't thought about it much but "maybe I should pass it to my children." As Casey quips, this turned the world's most consequential AI company into a potential hereditary monarchy across "all 14" Musk kids. #4 is Helen Toner's recorded deposition dragging then-CTO Mira Murati for being "strikingly unsupportive" and "remarkably passive" during the November 2023 firing of Sam Altman — capped by Toner's instantly quotable line: "She was waiting to see which way the wind would blow, and she didn't realize that she was the wind."

#3 is Greg Brockman's "immortal diary" — entries the OpenAI president was required to submit as trial exhibits, in which he wrote about his ambitions to build AGI, hit a billion-dollar net worth, and be respected for "what I do." The hosts get into a bit about whether Musk's lawyers calling it a "diary" instead of a "journal" is a sexist swipe, and Kevin offers operational advice: "A bound book with a lock on it like you had when you were a pre-teen... You have to burn it after reading. That's on page one." #2 is the "Jackass Trophy" — an actual trophy that a young Dario Amodei presented to an OpenAI employee Musk had called a jackass during a meeting, engraved "Never stop being a jackass for safety." OpenAI is reportedly trying to use this as evidence that Musk didn't really care about safety; Casey is unconvinced ("this feels like a legal strategy supplied by ChatGPT") but supports the trial mainly as "a way to smuggle a bunch of gossip into the public record."

#1 is Ilya Sutskever's testimony, which the hosts call the spiciest moment. The former OpenAI chief scientist talked in riddles and parables, telling the judge that the difference between AI a decade ago and AI today is "like the difference between an ant and a cat," and responding to a question about OpenAI's compute needs with the now-iconic phrase: "If there's no funding, there is no big computer." Casey closes with a Hollywood-twist favorite — the revelation that Shivon Zilis, longtime Musk employee, mother of four of his children, and former OpenAI board member, had been functioning as Musk's covert liaison inside OpenAI. Kevin's deeper takeaway: in 2017, before any of this stuff really worked, all the players were writing down predictions about AGI dictators and global governance fights — and they were essentially correct about everything, even as they came to hate each other. He suspects this may be "the last great deposit of receipts" because today's executives have learned to communicate only via Signal disappearing messages.
