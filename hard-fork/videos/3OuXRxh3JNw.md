# Is Google Doing Enough to Win the A.I. Race?

## Summary

In this short bonus episode, Kevin Roose and Casey Newton record live from Google I/O 2026 — the company's annual developer conference — to share their immediate impressions of the keynote. Google announced what it calls the biggest change to Search in 25 years, a wave of new agentic AI products, and Gemini 3.5 Flash, a faster and cheaper model aimed at serving billions of users. Demis Hassabis closed the keynote by declaring that we are standing in the "foothills of the singularity," prompting Kevin to joke that by I/O 2027 we'll be at "base camp."

The hosts return to the studio after spending hands-on time with Gemini 3.5 Flash and Google's new anti-gravity coding assistant. Kevin reports that the model is fast and offers generous token limits, but doesn't feel like a meaningful improvement over existing frontier models — and the rollout is being further dampened by complaints about the cost, which is significantly higher than the previous Gemini Flash version. Casey notes that this efficiency-focused class of models has never really been for him: he'd rather wait a little longer for the best answer than get an instant cheap one.

Their overall verdict on I/O is "a base hit" — a solid set of announcements that proves Google has regained its footing in the AI race, but no killer feature that would force a power user to switch. The real test, they say, comes next month when Google ships Gemini 3.5 Pro, which is expected to compete at the actual frontier. Notably absent: any boos for AI mentions, which has been the dominant reaction at recent gatherings — the I/O crowd seemed actively excited about new AI capabilities, in stark contrast to college commencements and other public events.
